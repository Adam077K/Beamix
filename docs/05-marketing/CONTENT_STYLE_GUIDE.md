# Beamix — Content Style Guide
*The writing standard for every piece of Beamix content.*
*v1.0 · 2026-04-14 · Owner: Growth Lead*

---

## Who this guide is for

Every person or tool that produces content for Beamix: blog writers, editors, AI writing tools (as the prompt baseline), social media, email, and ad copy. If words go out with the Beamix name on them, this guide applies.

When in doubt: read the sentence aloud. If it sounds like a press release, rewrite it. If it sounds like something a smart friend would say, publish it.

---

## 1. Beamix voice — three words, concrete definitions

### Authoritative
We know this material. We have scanned thousands of SMB businesses. We have the data. We state things directly and don't hedge when we have evidence.

**Sounds like:** "FAQPage schema makes you 3.2× more likely to appear in Google AI Overviews. Add it before you publish another word."

**Does not sound like:** "It may be beneficial to consider adding FAQPage schema, as research suggests it could potentially improve AI Overview visibility."

### Direct
We get to the point in the first sentence. We don't warm up, we don't contextualize. We say the thing.

**Sounds like:** "Your business is invisible in ChatGPT. Here's why and what to fix."

**Does not sound like:** "In today's evolving digital landscape, AI search has become increasingly important for businesses of all sizes."

### Warm
We are talking to a real person who runs a real business and has real problems. We use "you" and "your business." We're on their side. We don't lecture.

**Sounds like:** "If you're reading this and you run a law firm in Tel Aviv, here's the part that matters for you specifically."

**Does not sound like:** "Business owners must take proactive steps to ensure optimal visibility."

## 4. Sentence structure rules

### Length variance — mandatory
Every paragraph must have sentence-length variety. If every sentence is 18-22 words, the paragraph is robotic.

**Target mix:** At least one sentence under 8 words, at least one over 25 words, in every 4-sentence block.

**Correct:**
> ChatGPT uses Bing's index for live web queries. It scans the top 20-30 results for a given search, reads the content, and re-ranks those sources by its own internal criteria — readability, factual precision, structural clarity — before synthesizing an answer that cites the most extractable passages. That means Bing ranking matters for ChatGPT visibility. Not Google.

**Wrong:**
> ChatGPT is an AI search engine that uses Bing's search index to find relevant content for user queries. It evaluates the top results and selects the most appropriate sources to include in its synthesized response. This means businesses need to ensure their websites rank well in Bing search results to appear in ChatGPT answers. Understanding this mechanism is important for developing an effective AI search strategy.

### Use sentence fragments deliberately
Fragments add rhythm and emphasis. Use 1-2 per article.

Good: "Most GEO advice is wrong for SMBs. Not wrong in theory. Wrong for the actual problem."
Good: "Invisible in AI search. Every day."

### One-line paragraphs
Use for maximum emphasis. Max 2 per article.

### Don't open with subordinate clauses
Wrong: "When considering the importance of AI search visibility for small businesses, it's clear that..."
Right: "Small businesses are losing customers to AI search. Most of them don't know it yet."

### Burstiness target
Humans write irregularly. Target a range of 4-45 words per sentence within any 500-word section. Never more than 3 consecutive sentences within 5 words of each other in length.

## 5. GEO formatting conventions

These are not style preferences — they are structural requirements for AI citation lift. [Source: GEO_MECHANICS.md §2, §3]

### TL;DR placement
Every article over 1,500 words gets a TL;DR block at the top. Format:

```
**TL;DR:** [One sentence summary of the article's main finding or recommendation. 
State the key number or takeaway.]
```

The TL;DR is the most-extracted passage by AI engines. Make it self-contained and quotable.

### Stat density
Minimum 1 sourced statistic per 300 words. Not approximate — exact sourced numbers with citation link.

Format: "A [date] study by [Author/Source] found that [specific number + specific outcome]."

Never: "Research shows that AI search is growing." Always: "Google AI Overviews now appear on more than 50% of queries as of October 2025, up from 6.5% in January 2025. [Source: The Digital Bloom, 2025]"

### Quotation usage
Every article over 1,000 words includes at least one direct quotation from a named source. Not "experts say." A real person, real name.

Format: `"[Exact quote]" — [Full Name], [Title/Credential], [Year]`

This produces a +28% citation lift in generative answers (Princeton GEO paper, KDD 2024).

### "Last updated" timestamp
Every article shows both publication date AND last-updated date at the top, directly under the title.

Format: `By [Author Name], [Credential] · Published [YYYY-MM-DD] · Last updated [YYYY-MM-DD] · [N min read]`

### Author byline + Person schema
Every article has a named human author with credentials. No exceptions. The byline links to the author's archive page at `/authors/[slug]`. The author archive page has Person schema with sameAs links (LinkedIn, X).

74% of sites hit by Google's Helpful Content Update lacked author bylines. (Lily Ray, Amsive, HCU analysis of 500 sites.)

### Definition block
Every article includes a clear, self-contained definition of its core concept in the first 400 words. Format it as a standalone paragraph, not a list item. This is the passage LLMs extract as the "what is X" answer.

Format:
> **[Concept] is [definition in 2-3 clear sentences. State what it is, not what it does. No hedging.]**

## 6. Headline formulas

Use one of these patterns for every H1. Test two options for each pillar article before publishing.

**Pattern 1 — Question (GEO lift):** "How [Specific Audience] [Achieves Specific Outcome] in [Year/Timeframe]"
Examples: "How Law Firms Get Cited by ChatGPT — The 2026 Visibility Playbook" / "How Restaurants Show Up on Perplexity in 2026"

**Pattern 2 — We-tested:** "We [Tested/Scanned/Ran] [Specific Thing]. Here's What We Found."
Examples: "We Scanned 1,000 Israeli SMBs Across 4 AI Engines. Here's What We Found."

**Pattern 3 — Specific problem:** "Why [Specific Thing] [Fails/Disappears/Doesn't Work] — And What Fixes It"
Examples: "Why Restaurants Disappear on AI Search — And What to Fix This Week"

**Pattern 4 — Number + specific outcome:** "The [N]-Day [Specific Action] for [Specific Audience]"
Examples: "The 60-Day GEO Checklist for Any Local SMB" / "The 90-Day ChatGPT Visibility Plan for Moving Companies"

**Pattern 5 — Contrarian:** "[Common belief]. [It's wrong / That's changed / Here's why]."
Examples: "Most GEO Advice Is Wrong for Small Businesses. Here's What Actually Works."

**Pattern 6 — Specific insight:** "[Specific Audience] + [AI Engine]: The [Pattern/Finding/Fix] That [Specific Outcome]"
Examples: "Real Estate Brokers + AI Search: The Listings-That-Rank Pattern"

**Pattern 7 — Cost/stakes:** "[Specific Consequence] — [Specific Cause]"
Examples: "What Your Business Loses Every Month It's Invisible in AI Search"

**Headline rules (from BRAND_GUIDELINES.md §9):**
- 6-10 words target (can push to 12 for vertical specifics)
- No exclamation points
- Present tense or imperative
- Include the primary keyword naturally
- No "ultimate guide to [vague noun]" patterns

## 7. SMB vertical adaptations

Beamix serves specific verticals. Voice and framing shift for each, even though the underlying principles are the same. Here's how:

### Law firms
- Use precise legal language (not dumbed-down). Partners read this.
- Frame in terms of client acquisition and referral pipeline, not "leads"
- Numbers they care about: new matter intake, consultation booking rate
- Their vocabulary: "matter," "client intake," "referral," "citation" (legal sense too — use carefully)
- Opening hook: a missed referral / a competitor getting the call

### Restaurants
- Frame in terms of covers, reservations, and foot traffic — not "leads"
- Their vocabulary: "covers," "walk-in traffic," "Yelp presence," "reservation platform"
- Seasonal urgency works: "before summer / before the holidays"
- Opening hook: "someone searched 'best Italian in [neighborhood]' and your restaurant wasn't in the answer"

### Insurance agents / brokers
- Frame in terms of quote requests and conversion — they track metrics closely
- Their vocabulary: "quote," "bind," "policy," "referral," "producer"
- They are skeptical of technology promises — lead with data, not benefits
- Opening hook: a competitor ranking for a specific coverage query

### Moving companies
- Frame in terms of booked jobs and call volume — not "impressions" or "visibility"
- Seasonal urgency is extremely high (spring/summer peak)
- Their vocabulary: "booked moves," "inbound calls," "local jobs vs long-distance"
- Opening hook: "Someone asked ChatGPT for a moving company in [city] this week. Here's who came up."

### Dentists
- YMYL-adjacent — be precise, cite sources, avoid health claims
- Frame in terms of new patient appointments, not "leads"
- Their vocabulary: "new patients," "scheduling," "insurance accepted," "specialty"
- Opening hook: a patient's search journey from ChatGPT to booking

### Real estate brokers
- Frame in terms of listings, buyer/seller inquiries, and market position
- Their vocabulary: "listing," "inquiry," "neighborhood," "days on market," "seller side"
- They care deeply about local market specificity — use city and neighborhood names
- Opening hook: a buyer asking ChatGPT for a broker and not finding them

### Trades (plumbers, electricians, contractors)
- Frame in terms of inbound calls and booked jobs — they are busy, not into analytics
- Short sentences, plain language, specific dollar amounts
- Their vocabulary: "call volume," "dispatch," "emergency calls," "service area"
- Opening hook: "Emergency search. Your number didn't come up. Competitor got the call."

## 8. Hebrew voice note

For the future Hebrew content pass — this is not a translation guide. It is a tone brief.

Hebrew Beamix voice is: **ישיר, מקצועי, ישראלי.** (Direct, professional, Israeli.)

- No formal academic Hebrew — business casual register (דיבורי עסקי)
- Israeli business owners are direct. Don't soften.
- Use Hebrew city names naturally (תל אביב, ירושלים, חיפה — not Tel Aviv in Hebrew text)
- Contractions and shortened forms are fine — do not translate from English word-for-word
- The word "לבסוף" is the Hebrew equivalent of "in conclusion" — avoid it
- The phrase "חשוב לציין" is the Hebrew "it's important to note" — avoid it
- Numbers stay in numerals even in Hebrew: 41%, 3.2×, $49
- Hebrew content is NOT a translation. It is written fresh in Hebrew with Israeli SMB examples.

## 9. Pre-publish reviewer checklist

Run this check before any article goes live. Every item is a hard gate — article does not ship until all are checked.

### E-E-A-T signals
- [ ] Real human byline with credential (not a pseudonym, not "Beamix Team")
- [ ] Author links to author archive page at `/authors/[slug]`
- [ ] Author archive page has Person schema with sameAs links
- [ ] Last-updated date visible at top of article
- [ ] First-hand experience signal present ("I tested / We scanned / When [Real Business] ran...")
- [ ] At least 2 outbound links to authoritative sources (.gov, .edu, recognized publications, primary research papers)
- [ ] At least 1 original data point or observation from Beamix's own scanning

### GEO structure
- [ ] TL;DR block at top (articles 1,500w+)
- [ ] At least 1 sourced statistic in first 100 words
- [ ] At least 1 direct named quotation
- [ ] Self-contained definition block in first 400 words
- [ ] Sourced stat per 300 words throughout (minimum 1 per 300w)
- [ ] Internal links to ≥3 related Beamix articles
- [ ] CTA to free scan or relevant article is present and specific

### Schema
- [ ] Article / BlogPosting schema validates in Rich Results Test
- [ ] FAQPage schema present if article has Q&A block
- [ ] BreadcrumbList schema present
- [ ] Person schema on author reference
- [ ] All schema validates without errors at https://search.google.com/test/rich-results

### Banned words
- [ ] Document has been searched for every word on the banned list (§3)
- [ ] No instance of any banned word remains in the published version
- [ ] Reading aloud check: reads at natural conversational pace without robotic rhythm

### Readability
- [ ] Reading level target: Grade 8-10 (Hemingway App or equivalent)
- [ ] No paragraph longer than 4 sentences without a break
- [ ] Sentence-length varies within every paragraph (mix short and long)
- [ ] No sentence-length uniformity over 3 consecutive sentences

### AI detector sanity check
- [ ] Runs through Originality.ai or equivalent — target score: <30% AI
- [ ] If score is >50%: identify the worst 3 paragraphs and rewrite before publishing
- [ ] This is a sanity check on quality, not a ranking gate — Google does not use detectors

### Meta / SEO
- [ ] Title tag: 50-60 characters, primary keyword included
- [ ] Meta description: 140-160 characters, includes primary keyword, reads as a benefit statement (not a list of words)
- [ ] OG image: 1200×630px, under 300KB
- [ ] Featured image alt text written (descriptive, not keyword-stuffed)
- [ ] URL slug: lowercase, hyphenated, primary keyword present, under 60 characters

### Framer publishing
- [ ] Cover image set and displays correctly in Framer preview
- [ ] Excerpt field filled (= meta description)
- [ ] Author field selected from Authors collection
- [ ] Published Date AND Updated Date set
- [ ] FAQ Items populated if applicable (for dynamic FAQPage schema)
- [ ] Submitted to Search Console + Bing Webmaster after publish
