# Frame 4 — User Researcher / Behavioral Expert Seat
*Board review of Frames 1, 2, 3 + Frame 4 proposal grounded in real Sarah/Yossi behavior*
Date: 2026-04-26
Seat: User Researcher (15+ yrs ethnographic with SMB owners and small marketing teams)

---

## Preamble: the lens I am bringing

Before I critique anything, I want to anchor the conversation in two behavioral asymmetries we keep glossing over.

**First asymmetry — stated vs. revealed preference for "simplicity."** Every Sarah-type I have ever interviewed *says* "I want it simple, I don't have time, just do it for me." The same Sarah, two months in, opens her dashboard, sees a calm one-line summary, and quietly cancels because "I don't really know what they're doing for me." Her stated preference is simplicity; her revealed preference is **legible proof of work that her brain can metabolize in 30-60 seconds**. These are not the same thing. Frame 1 confuses them. Frame 3 is in danger of confusing them. The product that survives churn is the one that makes the work *visible without being noisy* — which is a different design problem than "show less."

**Second asymmetry — the price-checking paradox.** Sarah at $79 will check Beamix every other week and tolerate sparse signals. Sarah at $189 will check it every 3-5 days for the first 8 weeks and demand richer signals. Sarah at $499 (Yossi) will check daily and demand maximal signals. We are pricing into three behavioral cohorts and pretending one above-the-fold serves all of them. It does not. **Pricing tier is a behavioral variable, not just a feature gate.** I will return to this.

With those two anchors set, here is my critique.

## SECTION 1: Critique of the three frames

### Frame 1 — The Managed Service

**Most likely real usage pattern.** Sarah opens the app once in week 1 (curiosity), once in week 4 (skepticism check), then either churns at week 6-8 ("I'm paying $189 and I haven't logged in for a month — is this even working?") or never logs in again and lets it autorenew. Yossi rejects this frame entirely on day one because his entire economic model is *demonstrating* work to clients; a managed-service framing strips his deliverable surface.

**Cognitive load that doesn't pay off.** None — the load is correctly low. That's not the failure. The failure is **the load is too low**. Sarah has a 5-minute weekly cognitive budget for Beamix; if the product spends only 30 seconds of that budget, she fills the remaining 4.5 minutes with anxiety about whether her money is working. Frame 1 assumes saved cognitive load is pure win. It's not. Some of it is "cognitive load looking for a target" and the target becomes "is this product real?"

**Where it under-delivers on perceived value.** Catastrophically. Sarah pays $189 because she has internalized that $189/mo means "real software with real work happening." A homepage that says "Healthy and gaining" + a number + nothing else is indistinguishable in feel from a free tool. The tool is doing more work than ever; the *legibility* of that work is at the lowest point in any frame. Sarah's brain runs the equation "feels like = is" and concludes "feels like a free tool, must be a free tool's worth of value." She downgrades or cancels.

**Abandonment risk.** Highest of the three frames. Behavioral pattern: weeks 2-6 are the danger zone for SaaS where the user lacks internal evidence of work. Frame 1's email-as-relationship is real, but emails get archived, swiped, and forgotten. What survives is the felt sense of a tool you respect — and you can't respect a tool you never see.

**Trust mechanism — stable?** Fragile. Trust here = "I trust them enough to not check." That trust is built on emails and a pristine empty surface. ONE bad email — one digest where the score dropped and the explanation was vague — and the entire trust structure collapses, because there is no other surface where Sarah can go to rebuild her sense of what's happening. The product has put all its trust eggs in one basket (the digest) and given users no fallback verification surface that doesn't feel like "extra work."

**R3 onboarding patterns honored/ignored.** Honored: Vercel, Granola, ChatGPT — anti-ceremony, immediate work. Ignored: Linear's "you are working in 60 seconds with visible artifacts," Notion's "the medium is the message" — both depend on **visible product surface as proof.** Frame 1's onboarding succeeds in the first 90 seconds and then runs out of mechanism for week 2.

**Email-vs-app channel logic.** The logic is that email is primary because users "live in inbox." This is half-true. Users live in inbox for *messages*, not for *dashboards*. The Monday Digest is a message. But Sarah's question "am I getting my money's worth?" is a dashboard question, not a message question. Frame 1 forces a dashboard question into a message channel. Wrong fit.

### Frame 2 — The Operating Studio

**Most likely real usage pattern.** Yossi loves it for 3 weeks, then starts skipping the artisanal moments because his throughput is 47 items/day and Excalifont chip-chrome at scale is a 2-3 second tax per interaction. Sarah opens it once, gets a vibe she doesn't recognize ("this looks like a Moleskine for nerds"), feels alienated, and never returns. The aesthetic register signals *"this product expects you to be a craftsperson"* — and Sarah is not a craftsperson, she's an ops manager who hates being made to feel illiterate.

**Cognitive load that doesn't pay off.** Most of it. The Notebook, Reasoning Receipt typed-in, Sentence Builder chip-in-prose, card-flip mechanic, Letter on settings — every one of these is asking Sarah to learn a *vocabulary* before she can use a *function*. This is the "fluency tax" I warned about. Vercel charges no fluency tax. Linear's onboarding teaches itself by being used, not by being decoded. Frame 2 makes the product the puzzle.

**Where it under-delivers on perceived value.** Counterintuitively, it under-delivers on *legibility*. A Notebook in Excalifont feels personal but is 3x slower to scan than a clean table. Yossi's clients ask "what changed this week?" and Yossi has to *read* the Notebook to answer. He hates that. Sarah opens Profile-as-Letter and doesn't know where to update her email. Craft becomes a wall between user and answer.

**Abandonment risk.** Sarah: high (week 1-2 alienation). Yossi: medium-low after Phase 1 (he adapts because he's a power user) but high in Phase 2 if a competitor with cleaner ops eats his lunch on speed.

**Trust mechanism — stable?** Mixed. The Reasoning Receipt is genuinely a trust-building artifact — it's *one* of the strongest moves in any frame. But it's buried under 11 other distinctive moves competing for protagonist status. The trust-builder gets diluted.

**R3 patterns honored/ignored.** Ignored almost entirely. R3's lesson is *the product is the onboarding*. Frame 2's product is a small museum the user has to *learn how to be inside.* The horizon-path-draw onboarding is a beautiful one-time moment that tells Sarah "you're entering a special place" — but Sarah doesn't want a special place; she wants a tool. Wrong meta-frame.

**Email-vs-app channel logic.** Frame 2 is app-primary; email is incidental. Wrong for Sarah, fine for Yossi. The frame implicitly assumes one user; both fail differently.

### Frame 3 — Calm by Default, Depth on Demand (currently locked)

**Most likely real usage pattern.** Sarah: opens once in week 1, sees "Healthy and gaining" + the number, scrolls a tiny bit, closes. Week 4: opens it, sees the same calm sentence, doesn't scroll, closes faster. Week 6: thinks "what am I paying for?" — opens it, sees the calm sentence + number + maybe a small change in score, doesn't know what the change means, closes confused. Week 8: cancels OR forgets to cancel. **The calm-by-default surface starts to feel like a placeholder.** Yossi: uses /inbox heavily, ignores /home, goes deep on /scans and /competitors, lives in the depth scroll. The frame works for him. It does not work for Sarah at $79-189 in the way the spec assumes.

**Cognitive load that doesn't pay off.** The above-the-fold load is correctly minimal. But the **navigational** load is hidden: Sarah has to *decide whether to scroll* on every visit. Decision-making is itself a load. Frame 3's "depth on demand" is great in principle but in practice asks Sarah to take an action (scroll) to get the proof of value she needs. She won't. She'll close the tab.

**Where it under-delivers on perceived value.** The above-the-fold is *too restrained for $189-$499*. "Healthy and gaining" + a 72px number is what a $19/mo wellness app shows. The visual signature is correct in spirit but is calibrated for the wrong tier. Behavioral evidence: when premium B2B SaaS shows "everything is fine" too minimally, users price-anchor down. Linear's "all caught up" works because the user just *did* the work and the empty state is the reward. Beamix's calm state has the user doing nothing — so the empty state is not a reward, it's a void. **Different psychological context, same UI move, opposite effect.**

**Abandonment risk.** Medium-high for Sarah at the $189 tier. Lower at $79 (the price tolerates lower legibility). Lowest for Yossi at $499 (he uses depth scroll).

**Trust mechanism — stable?** Better than Frame 1 (depth scroll is the verification surface), worse than I'd want. The Status Token + Score Number is doing too much trust work alone. One bad week — score drops, single sentence reads "Score dipped — we're acting" — and Sarah's anxiety has nowhere to land because the immediately-visible information is too sparse to soothe her. She has to scroll to depth to find reassurance, but in an anxious moment she's *less* likely to scroll, not more — anxiety narrows attention.

**R3 patterns honored/ignored.** Honored beautifully on onboarding (Vercel pattern, 2 steps + magic moment, first agent live). Honored on /scan public (high-craft acquisition). **Ignored on /home steady-state.** Linear's home isn't sparse — it shows your active issues with structured density. Vercel's dashboard isn't sparse — it shows deployments with rich metadata. The R3 reference set is "calm" in tone, NOT "minimal" in information. There is a distinction. Frame 3 conflated them.

**Email-vs-app channel logic.** Frame 3's "Monday digest IS the relationship" is overstated. Email is *one* relationship surface. The app is another. The product loses if either is anemic. Frame 3 makes the digest the headliner and reduces the app to escape-hatch. For Yossi this is wrong; for Sarah this is *theoretically* right but practically risky for the reasons above.

## SECTION 2: Frame 4 proposal — "Calm surface, dense proof"

The instinct behind Frame 3 is correct. The execution is over-corrected. Frame 4 keeps Frame 3's *philosophical* foundation — calm tone, depth on demand, email as a real channel — but rebuilds the surface so that **the proof of value is metabolizable in the first 30 seconds of a Sarah visit, without scrolling, without learning a new vocabulary, and without manufacturing fake busywork.**

The headline reframe: **calm is the tone, dense is the information architecture.** A calm surface can carry more information than Frame 3 allows; what makes it calm is the visual hierarchy and the lack of urgency, NOT the absence of content. Stripe Dashboard is calm. Linear is calm. Both are *information-dense*. Frame 3 has confused calm with sparse.

I'll structure Frame 4 by addressing the prompt's questions in behaviorally-grounded order.

### The "moment of truth"

Every Sarah session has exactly one moment that determines whether she renews. It is **the first 8 seconds after the page loads.** If in those 8 seconds she sees evidence that *specific work happened on her behalf since her last visit*, she relaxes. If she sees a status sentence and a number, her brain reads "marketing veneer" and she tenses.

The moment of truth is not "did the score go up." It is "**is there a concrete artifact of work I can point to?**" — even one. "Citation Fixer added 11 FAQ entries on Tuesday." That sentence does more trust work than any score number. It's specific, dated, and tangible. Frame 4 puts at least one of these in the above-the-fold on every visit.

For Yossi, the moment of truth is also at 8 seconds, but the question is different: "**what changed since I left?**" — a delta question, not a proof question. Yossi already trusts; he wants the operational summary fast.

One above-the-fold has to deliver both. Frame 4 design: a **three-row above-the-fold** (not three columns, not a single sentence). Row 1: status sentence + score + delta (Frame 3's signature, kept). Row 2: "**this week's evidence**" — 1-3 specific artifacts, each one line, each timestamped, each with a click-through. Row 3: the conditional decision card (kept from Frame 3).

This is denser than Frame 3 but still calm. Because rows are visually rhythmic, readable in any order, and the eye reads them in 8-12 seconds. Sarah leaves feeling proof. Yossi leaves feeling oriented.

### /home above-the-fold

The Frame 4 above-the-fold is structured as follows. I'll be specific because the design moves matter.

**Row 1 — Status & Score (height ~120px)**
- Left: status sentence in InterDisplay 22px + colored Status Token (Frame 3's three-state token, kept).
- Right: score number in tabular nums 56px (smaller than Frame 3's 64-72px — make room for row 2 above the fold), delta chip.
- Single subtle line below: "vs. last week" timestamp.

**Row 2 — Evidence Strip (height ~180px) — THE NEW MECHANIC**
- Three "evidence cards" in a horizontal row, each 1/3 width.
- Each card: agent name + verb + object + timestamp + tiny status pill. Example:
  - *"Schema Doctor fixed 3 markup errors on /pricing — Tue 10:14am — applied"*
  - *"Citation Fixer added 11 FAQ entries — Wed 4:02am — applied"*
  - *"Competitor Watch found you pulled even with Profound — Thu 8:30am — observed"*
- Click any card → expand inline (Frame 3 depth, but pulled above the fold).
- This is the "concrete proof of work" Sarah needs in 8 seconds. It is **not** a notebook, **not** a Reasoning Receipt typed-in, **not** chip-in-prose. It is three plain sentences with timestamps. Stripe-grade clarity.
- Empty week (rare): *"Quiet week — your crew ran 6 monitoring checks; nothing needed action. Next scan: Friday."* — preserves the felt sense of work even when no artifacts.

**Row 3 — Decision Card (conditional, height ~100px or 0px)**
- Frame 3's pattern, kept verbatim. ONE card, ONE button when needed; absent otherwise.

**Total above-the-fold:** ~400-450px (vs Frame 3's ~280px). Still calm — there are only three rows, all with strong visual hierarchy, no chrome, generous whitespace. But the *information density* is roughly 2.5x Frame 3.

This single change addresses 80% of my Frame 3 critique. **Calm tone, dense proof.**

### Decision pattern — handling "nothing for the user to do"

Frame 3's bet is that most weeks there's nothing to decide, and the user should see literal empty space where the decision card was. I disagree behaviorally. Here is the reframe.

The user should never see "nothing." The user should see "**we did the work and here is the proof**." The empty-decision state is not an absence; it is a presence of **completed action.** The Evidence Strip (Row 2) IS the decision-card-replacement when nothing needs the user. It is always there. Three slots. Three artifacts. Three pieces of week's-worth-of-work proof.

When a decision IS needed, the Decision Card appears in Row 3 *in addition to* the Evidence Strip — not instead of it. Sarah sees: "we did this work" AND "we need you on this." Both, every time, with the right cardinality.

This kills the "empty page on calm weeks" risk. Manufacturing fake busywork is forbidden. Showing real completed work that already happened is mandatory.

**One nuance for Yossi at Scale tier:** Row 2 should hold up to 5 evidence cards in a horizontal-scroll strip on Scale, with a "view all 12 this week" link. For Sarah on Discover/Build, three is plenty. Tier-aware density is correct because pricing-tier-as-behavioral-variable.

### Depth scroll

Frame 3 puts depth below the fold. Frame 4 *also* puts depth below the fold, but the role of the depth scroll changes because the above-the-fold now carries proof. Below the fold becomes:

- **Section 1: 12-week sparkline of score** with hover dots showing what each peak/trough corresponds to. (Not the headline — Sarah doesn't read trend charts in 30 seconds.)
- **Section 2: Per-engine quick-strip** (kept).
- **Section 3: Recent crew activity** (a condensed Inbox preview — last 5 actions, expandable).
- **Section 4: Competitor delta** (one row pulled from /competitors).
- **Section 5: Goal track** — Frame 3's onboarding goal becomes a sticky progress bar here.
- **Section 6: Next scheduled scan + next digest send**.

Sarah will likely never scroll past Section 2 in steady-state. That is fine — the proof is above the fold. Yossi will scroll to Section 4-5 daily; he'll skip 1-2 because he already gets that data on /competitors.

### Email digest cadence

Frame 3 says weekly Monday morning. I disagree on cadence and contents.

**Behavioral evidence:** Monday-only digests have a known failure pattern. Users archive them in the morning rush, never read them, and the relationship surface goes silent for 7 days. Worse: the *only* time the digest is most needed is when something *moves* — a score drop, a competitor gain, an action that needs review. Calendar-based cadence misses event-based moments.

**Frame 4 cadence proposal — hybrid:**
1. **Weekly Monday digest** — kept (Frame 3 contract). Always sends. Predictable. The "everything's fine" base layer of relationship.
2. **Event-triggered briefs** — new. When a meaningful event occurs (score moves >3 points either direction, a competitor pulls ahead, an agent flags something high-confidence), a *short* email goes out within 4 hours, not waiting for Monday. Format: 2 sentences + one CTA + link to /home. Throttle: max one event-brief per 48 hours per user, to avoid notification fatigue.
3. **Optional daily morning digest for Yossi (Scale tier)** — opt-in. Configurable per-client.

**Why this matters behaviorally:** Sarah at $189 needs to *feel* the product respond when reality changes. A weekly digest pretends reality moves on a calendar. It doesn't. Event-triggered email closes the trust loop on the worst-case scenario (score dropped) within hours instead of within a week.

### The user-crew connection — beyond digest

Frame 3 treats the digest as the relationship. I'd add three lighter-weight connection mechanics that don't burden Sarah:

1. **Activity timeline on /home Section 3** — a compact "your crew this week" timeline. Not a full Inbox. Just 5 lines: agent, action, timestamp, status. Sarah skims it in 4 seconds; Yossi clicks through.
2. **Per-agent micro-bio on hover** — anywhere an agent name appears in the product (Evidence Strip, Activity, Inbox), hovering for 600ms shows a tiny tooltip: name, role in 6 words, items completed this month. No card flip. No personality. Just enough humanization to make "Schema Doctor" feel like a teammate, not a feature.
3. **One named "lead agent" per goal track** — Sarah picked her goal in onboarding; one agent is named the lead on it. The digest references that agent by name first ("Citation Fixer is leading your AI-citations goal..."). This is the lightest possible personification — it doesn't require characters or mascots, just consistent attribution.

These three together give the felt sense of "I have a crew" without forcing Sarah to read a notebook or visit a /crew page she doesn't care about.

### Onboarding — the new "ah-ha" after Paddle

Frame 3's Vercel pattern is correct and I keep it. Two steps + first agent live. The "ah-ha" question is: what specifically does Sarah see in the first 90 seconds *after* payment that locks in trust?

R3 evidence: Linear shows you a working issue. Vercel shows you a live URL. Granola shows you an enhanced note. The pattern: the user's *own data* transformed by the product, visible immediately.

Frame 4 onboarding ah-ha: **"Your first scan is loading evidence."** Within 60 seconds of Paddle payment, Sarah sees on /home: ONE evidence card already populated — *"Schema Doctor finished reading your homepage. Found 3 schema issues. Fix queued."* — with a real, specific, on-her-domain finding. The agent ran *while she paid*. The first thing she sees post-payment is *not a "welcome to Beamix"* — it's *evidence Beamix already started working on her actual site*.

This is a stronger ah-ha than Frame 3's "your crew is on it." Frame 3's version is a *promise*. Frame 4's version is *delivered work*. Promise vs. delivery: the latter wins on first-90-seconds psychology by 4-5x in conversion data I've seen across B2B onboardings.

Tradeoff: this requires the scan engine + Schema Doctor to be fast enough to deliver one finding within 60-90 seconds. If that's not possible, fall back to Frame 3's pattern. But the engineering effort is worth it.

### /crew specifically — will Sarah ever visit?

No. Sarah will visit /crew once, in week 1, for curiosity. After that she'll never return. Yossi will visit /crew weekly to compose the white-label digest.

Given this, /crew is **a Yossi-primary surface**. It should be designed for him, not built as a museum for Sarah. Frame 3's "digest authoring tool" is correct.

**Frame 4 /crew rebuild:**
- For Sarah: a single /crew page that shows the 11 agents as a roster (name, role, this-week activity count, success rate, link to "How [Agent] works"). Functional. Stripe-table clarity. No card flips, no personalities, no Excalifont. Sarah's one visit takes 90 seconds and she leaves having met her crew.
- For Yossi: the same page + a top section *"Compose this week's client digest"* with per-client controls. White-label. His daily-driver surface for client-deliverable work.
- The Frame 3 mechanic of /crew = digest authoring is correct for Yossi. For Sarah, /crew = roster reference. **Same page, two roles, layered by tier.** Scale-tier users see the digest composer; Discover/Build users see only the roster.

The roster is not a museum because there's no decoration. It's a contributor list, like Linear's team page. Behavioral test: Sarah's session on /crew should resemble her session on a SaaS team page (60-90 sec scan), not a museum visit (3-5 min linger).

### Trust escalation pattern — week 1 vs month 6

This is the most important behavioral pattern Frame 3 misses entirely.

**Week 1 user (verifying everything):** wants to see receipts. Wants to expand every Evidence card. Wants to read the per-engine breakdown. Wants the activity timeline visible. Wants the Reasoning Receipt for every decision the agent made.

**Month 6 user (verifying nothing):** wants to glance, see green, leave. Resents UI that asks her to expand things she's already learned to trust.

Frame 4 builds this in: **the depth-on-demand behavior is gradient, not binary.** Specifically:

- Week 1-2: by default, the first Evidence card on /home is auto-expanded showing the agent's reasoning. Subsequent cards stay collapsed.
- Week 3-4: all Evidence cards default collapsed, but a "tip" appears: "Click any card to see what we did and why."
- Week 5+: all Evidence cards collapsed, no tip. Sarah has internalized that depth is available; product trusts her to use it.

This is implementable as a `weeks_since_signup` feature flag on the Evidence component. Tiny code change, large behavioral payoff. **The product should age with the user.**

Yossi is exempt — he gets the verbose default permanently because his use is operational, not trust-building.

### Anxiety control — when score drops

Frame 3's status sentence "Score dipped — we're acting" is, behaviorally, almost the worst possible message in an anxiety moment. It tells the user something bad happened AND that someone else (the crew) is doing something AND gives Sarah no way to grasp what or why without scrolling. "Acting" is a vague verb in an anxiety moment. Sarah's amygdala fills the gap with worst-case interpretation.

**Frame 4 anxiety pattern — "name the cause, name the response, name the timeline."**

When score drops, the status sentence becomes (example): *"Score dipped 4 points — your competitor Profound published 12 new comparison pages. Citation Fixer is generating responses. Expect recovery within 2 weeks."*

Three sentences worth of information in one sentence. Specifically:
- **Cause** ("Profound published 12 new comparison pages") — names the threat. Anxiety needs a target; vague threats produce more anxiety than identified ones.
- **Response** ("Citation Fixer is generating responses") — specifies which agent and what action. Frame 3 said "we're acting"; Frame 4 says who, what, how.
- **Timeline** ("expect recovery within 2 weeks") — bounds the anxiety in time. Open-ended worry compounds; bounded worry resolves.

This is the single biggest behavioral upgrade in any frame. It is also where Beamix can beat every dashboard competitor — they show the dip, they don't soothe the dip.

The Evidence Strip below the status during a dip should pivot to *response evidence* — what the crew is doing about it, not what they did last week. *"Citation Fixer started 6 hours ago — currently writing FAQ section for /pricing."* In-progress evidence is more reassuring than completed evidence during a crisis.

### The "is this worth $189?" loop

The behavioral question for retention is: what concrete proof does Sarah need to renew month after month?

Behavioral evidence from B2B SaaS retention studies: users renew when they can answer "what did this tool do for me this month?" with a concrete, specific, sayable-out-loud sentence. They cancel when they can't.

**Frame 4 designs explicitly for the "sayable sentence."** The Monthly Renewal Email (new — sent 7 days before billing date) contains:

> "This month, your Beamix crew shipped [N] specific changes. Here are the three biggest:
> • [Concrete change 1 with measurable outcome]
> • [Concrete change 2 with measurable outcome]
> • [Concrete change 3 with measurable outcome]
> Your AI search visibility moved from [X] to [Y]. Renewing on [date]."

This is not the Monday digest. This is the renewal-anchoring email. It exists for one purpose: give Sarah the sayable sentence she needs to justify the spend (to herself, to her boss, to her own Sunday-night anxiety). Without this, churn risk peaks at billing dates. With this, churn resolves before it starts.

The same content lives on a `/account/value` page in the app, accessible from settings. Yossi can also generate this for clients.

This is the single most important retention mechanic in Frame 4. None of Frames 1-3 has it explicitly.

### Summary — what Frame 4 changes vs Frame 3

| Mechanic | Frame 3 | Frame 4 |
|---|---|---|
| Above-the-fold density | Status + score + (decision) | Status + score + Evidence Strip + (decision) |
| Empty/calm week feel | Empty space | Three timestamped artifacts of work |
| Email cadence | Weekly only | Weekly + event-triggered + monthly renewal |
| Score-drop messaging | "We're acting" | Cause + response + timeline |
| Depth-on-demand behavior | Static | Gradient by user age |
| Onboarding ah-ha | "Your crew is on it" (promise) | First evidence card delivered (proof) |
| /crew design | Same for everyone | Tier-layered: roster for Sarah, composer for Yossi |
| User-crew connection | Digest only | Digest + activity timeline + named lead agent |
| Renewal mechanism | Implicit | Explicit monthly renewal email + page |

Frame 4 keeps Frame 3's tone, philosophical foundation, and ~85% of its component spec. The deltas are surgical, behaviorally-grounded, and retention-critical.

## SECTION 3: Open questions for the board

1. **For the PM:** the Evidence Strip requires every agent action to surface a one-line summary structured as "agent + verb + object + timestamp + status." This is cheap if agents already structure outputs that way; expensive if not. Can the agent output schema be standardized to support this without reworking the agent runtime? If yes, it's the cheapest big-impact change in Frame 4.

2. **For the Designer:** can the Evidence Strip be designed so that an empty week (rare) still looks intentional rather than empty? My proposed empty-state copy ("Quiet week — your crew ran 6 monitoring checks; nothing needed action") preserves felt-work, but the visual treatment matters. What's the design move that says "nothing happened, by design" without reading as "nothing happened, because product is broken"?

3. **For the Visionary:** the trust-escalation pattern (gradient depth-on-demand by `weeks_since_signup`) is behaviorally strong but quietly opinionated — it implies the product *infers* the user's trust state. Are we comfortable with the product making that inference, or should depth-default be user-configurable? My behavioral take: do not make it configurable; users don't know what they want here, and the inferred default is correct 90% of the time.

4. **For all seats:** the monthly renewal email + `/account/value` page is the single highest-impact retention mechanic in Frame 4. It is also the easiest to ship because it's compositional (it reuses Evidence cards). Should we treat it as Tier 1 foundation (build first) or Tier 4 (ship last)? My behavioral vote: Tier 1. Retention starts on day 1, not month 5.

5. **For the PM and Designer jointly:** the event-triggered email pattern (max one per 48 hours) requires a "meaningfulness" filter on score moves and competitor changes. Cheap version: hard threshold (>3 points). Smart version: agent classifier ("is this a moment Sarah needs to know about now?"). Which version ships first? I recommend the cheap version at launch and revisit smart in Phase 2.

## Closing note (the User Researcher's bottom line)

Frame 3's instinct — that Beamix should respect the customer's time, that the product should not require constant engagement, that the digest is a real channel — is **correct** and Frame 4 keeps all of it.

Frame 3's execution mistake is conflating *calm tone* with *sparse information*. Stripe Dashboard, Linear, Vercel — every one of Frame 3's reference points is calm-and-dense. None of them is calm-and-empty. Frame 4 corrects this by making the above-the-fold carry **legible proof of work** in three timestamped artifacts, every visit, every week.

The behavioral test for Frame 4: a Sarah who pays $189/mo, opens the app for 30 seconds in week 6, and walks away saying out loud (or thinking, or telling her boss): *"Schema Doctor fixed 3 errors on Tuesday, Citation Fixer added 11 FAQs Wednesday, my score is up 5."* That sentence is the renewal mechanism. Frame 1 and Frame 3 cannot produce that sentence in 30 seconds. Frame 4 can.

Build for the sayable sentence. Everything else is downstream.

— User Researcher seat
