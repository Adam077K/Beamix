# Beamix Design Review — Through Stripe's Lens
Date: 2026-04-28
Reviewer: Senior Designer applying Stripe's editorial restraint + Dashboard density + Press-grade artifact + brand-at-every-scale disciplines

> *"The best way to predict the future is to invent it."* — Alan Kay, quoted in Stripe Press' editorial colophon. The lens here is Stripe-as-composite: stripe.com's editorial restraint, dashboard.stripe.com's data discipline, Stripe Press' physical-object care, the API docs' split-view declarative seriousness, and the brand book's gradient-as-restrained-event. Beamix has named Stripe as the bar. This is what that bar costs.

---

## A — /security page audit vs stripe.com/security

The /security spec is the most ambitious surface in the system, and the only one that cites Stripe by name. Read it next to stripe.com/security with a clear eye and three things become obvious: the spec out-structures Stripe in places, under-evidences Stripe in others, and imports an emotional register Stripe rejected on purpose.

**On the two-column architecture.** Stripe's /security is one long, dense, single-column scroll because Stripe's reader cohort is uniformly engineering and reads top-to-bottom. Beamix's reader cohort is not. The 280px sticky left rail with anchor links is the right call for Aria + procurement reviewers + AI crawlers; vercel.com/security and linear.app/trust both validate this pattern. Stripe would not ship the rail because Stripe does not need it. Beamix does. **The spec wins this one.** What Stripe ships that Beamix is missing: a *contextual* H2 above the fold listing the page's certifications (Stripe shows PCI DSS Level 1, SOC 1, SOC 2, ISO 27001, HIPAA-ready, all rendered as logo + cert number). Beamix at MVP has none of these. The honest move is the section the spec already includes, but it should sit higher — `SOC 2 Type II target Year 1 Q4` as a top-of-page disclosure, not buried in §5. Trust through specificity. Stripe does not say "we're working on it"; Stripe says "we are PCI DSS Level 1 audited by Coalfire, attestation date 2024-09-12." Beamix at MVP cannot say that. So Beamix says exactly what's true: target date + auditor name once selected. The page should ship the auditor RFP open, not closed.

**On the 100-word cryptographic-primitive paragraph in §9.** This is the strongest piece of writing in the entire Beamix spec. The HMAC-SHA-256 + 60s TTL + draft-hash-binding paragraph is genuinely declarative — it reads like a paragraph from the Stripe API reference's idempotency-key discussion. Stripe's API docs are arguably better-designed than the dashboard; the docs are the company's thesis statement, and they are written in declarative present-tense sentences with the subject leading. Beamix's §9 paragraph passes that bar. **One word change, per Ive's note: "refuses to publish" should become "cannot publish."** Refuses is volitional; cannot is mechanical. Aria forwards "cannot." He hesitates over "refuses." This single word shift is Stripe-grade.

**On the sub-processors table.** Stripe's stripe.com/legal/dpa-sub-processors is a Notion-clean table with vendor, services, location, transfer mechanism, and DPA link — five columns, ~30 rows, alternating row tint, no decoration. Beamix's spec is structurally identical and arguably better — the data-category column ("Prompts only, zero retention") is more informative than Stripe's because it discloses the LLM-prompt question Stripe doesn't yet face at procurement scale. The table on cream paper is the question. Stripe's is on white. **Cream is wrong here.** Procurement reviewers screenshot this; the screenshot lands in their vendor-review ticket; the ticket viewer is a white surface. A cream-paper screenshot in a white-surface ticket reads as *Beamix's letterhead pretending to be a legal document*. White table, white page, declarative present-tense column headers. The cream is doing emotional work in a context where emotional work undermines trust. Rams was right to flag this; I agree, with one refinement — keep the closing seal (a dateline-stamp signal) but render the page itself white.

**On the "talk to security" CTA.** Stripe's pattern is `security@stripe.com` plus a PGP key on a separate page, plus the security.txt file at /.well-known/security.txt. Beamix's spec covers the email and references PGP-key-on-request; **add the security.txt file**. RFC 9116 standard, ~12 lines, consumed by every vulnerability-disclosure tool on earth. Stripe ships it. Procurement reviewers' tooling expects it. Free trust signal.

**On the downloadable PDF.** Stripe ships actual SOC 2 Type II reports + DPA agreements + PCI AOCs through their Trust Center on TrustArc (a third-party platform). The "downloadable PDF version of the page" Beamix ships is a different artifact — a typeset technical disclosure at editorial register. **The right framing: this is the Beamix Posture document, not a SOC 2 report.** Name it that. `Posture v1.0 · April 2026 · 6 pages`. When SOC 2 Type II ships in Year 1 Q4, the posture document gets a §0.1 line: "SOC 2 Type II report available under NDA — request via security@beamix.tech." Stripe's pattern, scaled to MVP-stage company. The posture document is honest scaffolding for the eventual Trust Center.

The /security page survives the Stripe comparison narrowly. Move the page to white, ship security.txt, fix "refuses" to "cannot," surface the SOC 2 target above the fold, and rename the PDF the *Posture document*. Then it ships at Stripe's bar.

## B — Monthly Update PDF as Stripe Press

This is where Beamix has been most ambitious and most under-evidenced. "Stripe-Press-grade" is a specific claim, and Stripe Press is a specific operation: cream paper, sewn binding, embossed cover marks, a defined typeface stack (the books use a custom Stripe-commissioned typeface, not Fraunces), Sans Forgetica appears nowhere — that's a different category-error. The actual Stripe Press stack is **Söhne** for sans, **Söhne Mono** for code, and **GT Sectra** as the serif on titles. Custom-licensed; not retail Google Fonts. So before the spec is reviewed, the framing must be corrected: Beamix is building *Stripe-Press-grade composition with retail typefaces*, which is achievable but means every typographic choice carries 2× the load it would carry with a custom face.

**The cream paper hex.** Ive flagged this and was right: #F7F2E8 was guessed, not chosen. Stripe Press' actual paper — the trade hardback edition of *Working Backwards*, *The Score Takes Care of Itself*, *Poor Charlie's Almanack* — is a Cougar Natural-equivalent that scans somewhere around **#F4ECD8** under D65 daylight, slightly warmer and slightly more saturated than Beamix's chosen hex. #F7F2E8 has a faint green cast under cool LED office lighting; #F4ECD8 holds yellow. The /security page currently shows white-paper-island spec blocks at #FFFFFF inside #F7F2E8 cream — at #F4ECD8 the contrast holds (4.6:1 unchanged for body text at #0A0A0A). **My recommendation: switch to #F4ECD8 across all cream-paper surfaces** (Monthly Update, /scan public, Monday Digest header strip, OG share card). Document the choice with three printed swatches viewed under three light conditions, per Ive's spec. Without that documentation, the hex is theatre. With it, it is the chosen colour. The difference is everything.

**Typography stack at editorial-PDF scale.** Inter + InterDisplay + Fraunces 300 + Geist Mono is a strong retail stack but it is not a Stripe Press stack. The closest Stripe-Press analog with retail equivalents would be: **Söhne → Inter (close), Söhne Mono → Geist Mono (close), GT Sectra → Fraunces 300 with opsz 144 + soft 100 (close in feel, different in voice)**. Fraunces is a more decorative, more *playful* face than GT Sectra; the spec uses Fraunces 300 italic to mark editorial moments, which works at hero scale but starts to feel cute at the cover-pull-quote level. **The fix: limit Fraunces 300 italic to closing signatures, ledes, and pull-quotes; promote Fraunces 300 *roman* (non-italic) to the headline duties currently shown as italic.** Fraunces roman with opsz 144 reads closer to GT Sectra than Fraunces italic does. The italic, used too liberally, registers as decorative; the roman registers as editorial. This single discipline shift moves the PDF closer to Stripe Press.

**6-page composition audit.**
- **Page 1 (Cover):** business name 64px Fraunces + 28px Fraunces italic pull-quote. The pull-quote is the lede — but it's competing with the business name for the cover's load-bearing element. Stripe Press covers have *one* dominant element (the title). Ive's analog: the title typography is the cover. Recommendation: drop the pull-quote to Page 2 as the deck under the lead-attribution headline; let the cover be business name + month + Activity Ring (96px) + closing seal. Three elements. The cover is the artifact's title page.
- **Page 2 (Lead Attribution headline):** the strongest page in the spec. 48px Fraunces with Geist Mono numerals embedded — *"47 calls and 12 form submissions came from AI search this month"* — is the page Sarah forwards. **Tufte was right that Page 2 should also carry data**: pair the headline with a 4-row strip of micro-charts (calls/day-30d, forms/day-30d, score/week-12w, citations/week-12w) at 120×80px each. The headline is the lede; the micro-charts are the supporting evidence. Both fit on the page. Stripe's Annual Letter format does this — a headline number above a row of supporting micro-data.
- **Page 3 (Score + trajectory):** under-loaded per Tufte. **Replace the single 480×120 sparkline with the 11-engine small-multiples grid** (4×3, one cell housing the aggregate). 11 sparklines × 96×40px reads at the same density as a Stripe transaction-volume breakdown. Stripe Dashboard does exactly this on the Payments page — small multiples by region, by card brand, by currency. Beamix's Page 3 should be the per-engine breakdown.
- **Page 4 (Action timeline):** prose action blocks are the wrong primitive. Replace with a small-multiples action timeline — date, monogram (letter, no color), 13px verb-noun sentence, 48×16px micro-bar of score impact. The reader scans the column of bars and sees which actions moved the needle. Tufte's recommendation lands; Stripe would call this an "events log with attribution."
- **Page 5 (narrative):** keep. One editorial page after Pages 2-4's density is a deliberate breathing moment. Stripe's annual letters do this — three pages of charts followed by one page of executive prose.
- **Page 6 (closing):** the seal at 32×32 closing. **Stripe Press doesn't use seals** — Stripe Press uses an embossed colophon mark on the back cover plus the printer's mark inside the back endpaper. The Beamix seal is closer to a Penguin Modern Classics colophon than to Stripe Press. That's not wrong; it's a different lineage. Rename the move: it's a *colophon*, not a seal. Re-render at the same geometry but spec it as a colophon mark. The signature line *"— Beamix"* in 28px Fraunces italic is the right closing.

**Lead-attribution as Page 1 lede or Page 2 headline?** Page 2 headline. Page 1 is the cover; Page 2 is the lede. This is the Stripe Annual Letter pattern: cover (one image), Page 2 (the headline number), Pages 3-5 (the evidence), Page 6 (the close). Beamix's spec already approximates this; tighten it.

The Monthly Update is one of two Beamix artifacts that have a chance at Stripe-Press-grade with the moves above. The other is the printable Brief, which I'll champion in §G.

## C — Stripe Dashboard discipline applied to Beamix

Stripe Dashboard is the gold standard for product UI density. Open dashboard.stripe.com, look at the Payments overview, count the elements per row: the Volume chart at the top (with a 7d/30d/90d/12m toggle that doesn't break the chart's continuity), the Today/Yesterday/Week-to-date triplet, the Successful payments table with 8 columns (created, amount, fee, net, customer, payment method, decline reason, status) at 32px row heights, the Recent disputes panel, the Top errors panel, the Country breakdown — eight distinct surfaces on one page above the fold at 1440px. **Beamix's /home above the fold has roughly four** (Activity Ring, Decision Card, Evidence Strip preview, Tier badge). Beamix is too sparse where Stripe is dense.

**Density vs whitespace.** The 120px hero stretch on /home is generous to the point of marketing-page; a Stripe Dashboard equivalent would tighten to 56-72px hero stretch and recover the room for one more data block above the fold. The Stripe rule isn't "more density"; it's "the page should accommodate the operator's eye scanning seven things in five seconds." Beamix's /home accommodates four. **Recommendation: collapse the Activity Ring + score cluster from 200px to 144px, recover 56px of vertical, surface the per-engine 11-sparkline strip above the fold.** Yossi (managing 12 clients) gets the operator density he expects. Sarah (single-business owner) still gets the score as the dominant element because the Ring is still the largest object on the page; she just gets the per-engine read for free. Same page, two reading rates, denser than current spec.

**Hover sparklines + drill-downs.** Stripe's Charts have hover crosshairs that read precise values, plus a click-through to a drill-down view with the same chart at full size, plus filterable subdimensions (by country, by card brand, by error code). Beamix's KPI Quartet has hover sparklines (good) but no drill-down. **Every sparkline should be clickable to its own page.** The 11-engine strip on /home — click ChatGPT, land on /home/engines/chatgpt with the full 12-week trajectory, the queries that triggered citations, the rank distribution. This is the Stripe pattern: every chart is an entry point.

**The per-engine strip on /home.** The Stripe equivalent is the per-product or per-region drill-in on the Payments page — each row is rich (sparkline + count + delta + chip), and the page accommodates 8-12 rows above the fold. Beamix's per-engine strip currently reads as 11 chips in a row. Push it harder: each engine is a row, with a 96×24 sparkline + score + delta + last-citation timestamp. 11 rows, 32px each = 352px of vertical real estate, fits below the Ring with room to spare. Yossi can read his entire per-engine state in 4 seconds.

**The 4 lens chips on /scans (Done/Found/Researched/Changed).** Stripe doesn't use chips like this — Stripe uses *segmented controls* for filtering (the 7d/30d/90d/12m pattern) and uses *count badges* inline with table headers when summarizing aggregates. The 4-lens pattern on Beamix is closer to a Linear filter UI than a Stripe one. **What Stripe would ship:** the 4 lenses as a segmented control above the table (single selection, not multi), with the count rendered inline as `Done · 6 / Found · 11 / Researched · 4 / Changed · 2` in tabular Geist Mono. Click a segment, the table filters to that lens. Click "All", you get the unfiltered view. This is the Stripe Payments status filter pattern — All / Successful / Failed / Pending — and it would ship faster, scale better, and look more native.

## D — Brand-at-every-scale audit

Stripe's gradient appears at every scale: stripe.com hero (full-bleed), the dashboard.stripe.com login screen (full-bleed background), the API docs cover (a thin gradient band), the press release templates (an accent line), the favicon (a single hue from the gradient, not the gradient itself — gradients don't survive at 16px). Same brand, scale-appropriate rendering at every surface.

**Activity Ring at 200px → 96px → 24×24 → 16×16.** At 200px (/home), the Ring works — gap is large, terminus dot is legible. At 96px (Monthly Update PDF cover), the Ring still works — gap is 30°, terminus dot reduces to 1.5px but holds. At 24×24 (/security topbar lockup), Kare was right: the Ring becomes the *Beamix sigil*, not the Activity Ring. The score frame disappears; what remains is the small cross/star mark next to the wordmark. **This is two different marks the spec is conflating.** At 16×16 (favicon), the Ring cannot survive. The favicon is the Seal/colophon mark, not the Ring. Lock this in spec: Ring = score frame at 96px+; Sigil = wordmark mark at 24px and below; Colophon = signing mark on artifacts. Three marks, three jobs, three sizes. Stripe handles this correctly: the gradient is a hero element; the favicon is the `S` glyph alone.

**The Beamix sigil/wordmark lockup.** /security spec shows "24px tall total" with "16px InterDisplay 500 wordmark + cross/star sigil mark" but doesn't lock proportional spacing. Kare flagged this; I'll repeat it: the relationship between mark and wordmark is the design. Spend a day on it. At 24px lockup, the mark width should be ≈ cap-height of the wordmark, gap should be ≈ ¼ cap-height, mark sits left of wordmark for left-to-right languages and right of wordmark for Hebrew (per the dual-language brand requirement). Document the Hebrew lockup explicitly — most Western design systems don't, and it shows in their RTL pages.

**Cream paper register at 7+ surfaces.** Currently: Monthly Update, /scan public, Monday Digest header, /security, OG share card, Brief, Workflow Builder Editorial cell. **Seven surfaces is one too many for a register that's meant to mark "this is an artifact you keep."** Rams cut /security, the Workflow cell, and arguably the Monday Digest header strip. Ive cut the Workflow cell. I'd cut /security (per §A) and the Workflow cell (per Rams) and keep the rest. Five surfaces: Monthly Update PDF, /scan public, Monday Digest header strip, Brief, OG share card. Each is something the customer keeps, forwards, or screenshots. The cream becomes the Beamix tell that travels through Sarah's email, Marcus's Slack, Yossi's white-label deck. Five surfaces, one register, one job. Stripe's gradient is on six surfaces total across stripe.com and dashboard.stripe.com — same scale of restraint.

**The 4-mark sigil system at every scale.** Per Kare: three marks + one container. Ring (score frame, 96px+), Sigil/Seal/Colophon (signing mark, 16px–32px), Monogram (agent identity, 12px–96px), Margin (the surface marks accumulate on, not a mark itself). Drop the Trace from the sigil family — it's a behavior of text, not an object. This taxonomy survives at every scale because each member has a defined size range and a defined job. The Stripe analog: the gradient is the brand at hero scale, the wordmark `S` is the brand at icon scale, the typography is the brand everywhere else. Three primitives, scale-appropriate.

## E — Long-form respect — the missing editorial artifact

Stripe writes Increment essays. Patrick Collison runs patrickcollison.com with reading lists, project pages, and essays at the depth of academic blog posts. The Stripe blog publishes engineering-deep essays on database migrations, Currency-of-record problems, and idempotency. The sum of this output is a *long-form editorial register* that signals "this company thinks." It is one of Stripe's most-defensible moats and one of the cheapest to ship.

Beamix has no long-form artifact at MVP. The Monthly Update is editorial-flavored but not editorial; it's a customer artifact. The /security page is reference, not editorial. /scan is interactive, not durable. There is a hole the size of the Stripe Blog where Beamix's editorial voice should live.

**The State of AI Search annual report was deferred to Year 1 Q4.** This is the wrong call. Move it to MVP-1.5 — three months out, not nine. Here's the argument: at MVP launch Beamix has run scans for ~50–200 customers, has per-engine citation data nobody else publishes, and has the editorial register already established (cream paper, Fraunces, Geist Mono data, the colophon close). The data exists; the format exists. What's missing is the editorial framing — a 24-page report titled *The State of AI Search 2026: How small businesses appear in ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, and Gemini.* Cream paper. Fraunces titles. Geist Mono data. Per-engine breakdowns. Per-vertical findings (legal-services see X, plumbing sees Y, dental sees Z). Published as PDF + permalink + OG card. Distributed through email + Hacker News + LinkedIn. It is the artifact that moves Beamix from "GEO tool" to "the company that understands GEO."

This is the Stripe move: ship the artifact that proves you've thought about the category harder than anyone else. Stripe's *Atlas* did this for "how to incorporate a Delaware C-corp in 2014." The State of AI Search 2026 does it for "how small businesses surface in AI search." Six weeks to write, two weeks to design, one week to publish. MVP+90 days, not MVP+9 months. The cream paper is already doing the editorial work; let it work for something that earns Beamix its long-form respect.

White surface, Inter throughout, Fraunces banished from the body, dateline-stamp ("Last reviewed APR 27 2026") in Geist Mono as the only signature the page needs. Keep the colophon mark at the close — that's the dateline-stamp signal — but render the page itself white. The white-paper-island spec blocks become un-needed because the entire page is white. Cut the register-shift inside a register-shift; let the page be one register. Stripe-grade simplicity.

**Ive says the cream-paper hex wasn't chosen.** Correct. Stripe Press uses something closer to **#F4ECD8** under D65 — warmer, more durable across screens. My recommendation: switch to #F4ECD8 across all five remaining cream-paper surfaces, document the choice with printed swatches under three light conditions, and lock it in the brand book. The work of choosing the colour is the work; the colour itself is the artifact.

**Tufte's AI Visibility Cartogram.** This is the move. The cartogram (50 queries × 11 engines = 550 cells, color-coded, single image, scannable in 5 seconds) is the *Beamix equivalent* of Stripe's Atlas centerfold. **Ship it on Page 4 of the Monthly Update.** Replace the prose action blocks with the cartogram. **Kare's seed-per-agent fingerprint as a function spine.** Stripe's brand has exactly this kind of generative function: the gradient is parameterized, with a defined hue range, saturation, and opacity curve, applied procedurally to surfaces. Lock the seed-to-path function as a brand decision, version-controlled, immutable across versions the way Apple's logo is immutable. The function is the company.

## G — The Stripe-grade Beamix

Beyond the surface-by-surface critique, what does Beamix look like if shipped at Stripe's quality bar across product + marketing + press? Specific moves:

**1 — The Posture document, the Atlas equivalent, and the Quarterly Letter.** Three editorial artifacts, all on cream #F4ECD8, all signed with the colophon, all distributed via permalink + PDF + OG card. The Posture is /security as a printable document (covered in §A). The Atlas equivalent is *The State of AI Search 2026* (covered in §E). The Quarterly Letter is a 4-page founder letter from Adam at the close of each quarter, summarizing what shipped, what was rejected, what was learned. Stripe's quarterly investor letters set the tone; Beamix's quarterly customer letter sets the relationship. Three artifacts, one cadence, one brand voice.

**2 — The printable Brief.** When the customer signs the Brief at the end of onboarding, offer — once, never again — a "Print this Brief" button. The Brief is laid out on a single A4 page in cream-paper editorial register. The 7% who print will pin the page to a wall. They will tell other founders why. Ive made this recommendation; I'll second it and extend: ship the Brief at *two* sizes — A4 portrait for customer wall, and a postcard-size A6 for desk reference. The postcard ships unsolicited in month 6, alongside the printed Monthly Update Ive proposed. Two physical artifacts, $14 in shipping costs, infinite Twitter mileage.

**3 — The Workflow canvas as page, not grid.** Per Ive: delete the dot grid. The Workflow canvas becomes a sheet of paper at #F4ECD8, with nodes floating on it like diagrams in a notebook. The 24px snap-to-grid stays as invisible math; the visible grid disappears. This is the single move that makes the Workflow Builder unmistakably Beamix's. With the dot grid, it's a competent DAG editor. Without it, it's the only DAG editor that feels like writing.

**4 — The dashboard as Stripe-grade operator UI.** Per §C: tighten /home density, make every chart a drill-down entry point, surface the 11-engine strip above the fold, replace the 4-lens chips with a segmented control. Yossi gets operator density; Sarah gets the headline read. Two reading rates, one page.

**5 — The API docs as a design surface.** Stripe's API docs are arguably better-designed than the dashboard. Beamix at MVP doesn't have a public API, but it has the Truth File JSON Schema, the audit log export schema, and the Brief schema. Publish all three at beamix.tech/schemas/ as a Stripe-API-docs-style split-view: the schema on the left, an example JSON document on the right, with annotations linking schema fields to product surfaces. This is one of the cheapest brand moves available — a static site generated from JSON Schema files, with a CI hook that regenerates on every schema change. Two weeks of work, infinite long-tail discoverability via developer search.

**6 — Editorial restraint as the dominant register.** Three registers: Artifact (cream #F4ECD8, signed, Fraunces 300 roman + Fraunces 300 italic ledes only, Geist Mono data), Working (white, Inter throughout, brand-blue chrome only), Disclosure (white, Inter throughout, Geist Mono dateline-stamps, no Fraunces, no cream). Ship every Beamix surface in one of three registers. Refuse a fourth. Every register has one mood, one job, one paper.

**7 — The signature in motion.** Per Kare: invert the seal-draw curve from 800ms-traced to 240ms-stamped + 100ms-settle + 200ms-ink-bleed. A seal is stamped, not traced. Ive's note ties in: the Seal is the signature; the pen-stroke wordmark in onboarding is redundant ceremony. Cut the pen-stroke. Let the colophon stamp be the signing.

**8 — One Cycle-Close Bell.** Per Ive: when the weekly scan completes and auto-fixes have shipped, the Activity Ring closes its 30° gap over 800ms, holds 600ms, and re-opens. The KPI sparklines settle into final positions with a 200ms ease. The status sentence rewrites itself once. The page becomes, for two seconds, a closing curtain. This is Beamix's Apple Watch ring-close — a small, unrequested confirmation that what was being tracked has been honoured. It's the moment that earns Sarah's Monday-morning attention forever.

These eight moves don't add features; they tighten existing ones to Stripe's bar. Each is two days to two weeks of work. Together they move Beamix from "promising MVP" to "the GEO product designed at Stripe's quality bar."

## H — The move Patrick Collison would link to

The single move that earns a tweet from Patrick Collison and a link from his Twitter is not the Activity Ring, not the cream paper, not the colophon. It is **the AI Visibility Cartogram, shipped as the Page 4 centerfold of the Monthly Update, also rendered as a public OG share card, also embedded as a static SVG on every /scans/[scan_id] page**.

Here's why this is the move. Patrick Collison links to two kinds of artifacts: (1) prose he respects (Tyler Cowen, Stripe Press releases, founder essays on first principles), and (2) data visualizations that show him something he didn't know existed. The cartogram is category 2. It shows, in one image, the entire AI search visibility frontier for a single business — 50 queries × 11 engines, color-coded by citation outcome, with competitor positions plotted in each cell. There is no GEO product in the world that ships this view. Beamix has the data; nobody else has assembled it.

Tufte's framing is correct: this is the John Snow cholera map of GEO. One image, two reading rates (5-second cluster recognition + 5-minute per-cell study), every fact direct-labeled, no legend, no decoration. The Patrick Collison version: ship the cartogram as the public artifact of a /scan completion. When Sarah runs her free scan, she gets a permalink at beamix.tech/scan/[scan_id] showing the cartogram. The OG card is a mini-cartogram. The image is screenshot-able, tweet-able, link-able. Sarah tweets the cartogram with the line "this is what AI search visibility looks like for a small plumbing company in Tel Aviv." The tweet shows up in Patrick's feed. He retweets. *That* is the artifact-level moment.

Beamix ships this for the cost of a small-multiples React component + a server-side OG renderer. Three engineering-weeks. The strategic effect is permanent: Beamix becomes the company that *invented* the visualization for AI search visibility. Every future GEO product will reference the Beamix cartogram the way payment-product designers reference the Stripe Dashboard. The cartogram is the move. Ship it before MVP launch, ship it on every scan, ship it on the Monthly Update Page 4, ship it as an OG image. The cost is small; the moat is permanent.
