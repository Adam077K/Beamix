# Sarah Chen, Chen Family Plumbing — Reading the Beamix Frame 4 docs

47 employees. Tel Aviv. Tuesday night, 10:14pm. Husband is asleep. I have 22 minutes before I'm asleep too.

Let me just say up front — somebody forwarded me your "Frame 4 proposal" and asked me to read it like a customer. So I'm reading it like a customer. Not like a marketing person. Not like a software person. Like the person who actually pays.

Be warned: I'm going to be rude. Not because I want to. Because this is what's actually in my head.

## Section 1: Reading the docs as Sarah

### "Your AI Visibility Crew" — first impression

Okay. I read this line: *"Your AI Visibility Crew. Eleven AI specialists working autonomously to make sure your business shows up when customers ask AI engines about you."*

First reaction — what is "AI engines." I know ChatGPT. My nephew uses it. I tried it once for a customer email and it was fine. I don't know what "AI engines" means as a category. Is that ChatGPT plus other ones? Which ones? Why eleven? Why not three?

Second reaction — "AI Visibility Crew" sounds like a product name made by people who like product names. I would never say that out loud to my husband. "Honey, I just hired an AI visibility crew." He would laugh.

Third reaction — "show up when customers ask AI engines about you." Okay, this part I get. Sort of. My friend Yaakov said his grandson asked ChatGPT for a plumber and it gave him a name that wasn't ours. That stuck with me. That actually scared me. So if you're saying "we make sure your name comes up when somebody asks ChatGPT for a plumber" — yes. Say THAT. Don't say "visibility crew." Say "we get your name in ChatGPT."

I'm already three sentences in and my brain is tired. I would have closed the tab if a friend hadn't told me to read it.

### The 11 named agents

I scrolled. I saw a list. Schema Doctor, Citation Fixer, Competitor Watch, FAQ Agent, and seven others I don't remember.

Honestly? I don't care that there are 11. I would care if there were 1 and it worked. Eleven feels like you're trying to make me think I'm getting a lot. I bought a vacuum that said "12 attachments." I use 2.

The names are cute but they're a little embarrassing. "Schema Doctor." It's not a doctor. It's a script. Calling it a doctor is the same energy as Mailchimp calling its emails "campaigns." It's not a campaign. It's an email.

That said — if I had to remember which one fixed my website, "Schema Doctor" is easier than "module 7." So fine. Names — okay. Eleven — too many. Three would be better. "The one that fixes my site, the one that watches competitors, the one that writes things." Done. If you have eleven on the back end, fine, but show me three.

### The Standing Order

Wait. I have to sign a thing? In onboarding?

Let me re-read this. *"A short written agreement between the user and their AI Crew, in plain English, defining what the crew is for."*

I just signed up. I don't know what I want. I want my phone to ring more. That's what I want. If you make me write a "standing order" with you and I don't know what to write — I'm going to close the tab. This is the SEO consultant problem all over again. He made me fill out a "discovery questionnaire." I never finished it. He never started.

Okay then I read further — "Guided Assembler, NOT free-form text. 5-8 chip-menus that fill in the blanks of a sentence template." Okay that's better. Multiple choice I can do. I do multiple choice with my insurance.

But — the example sentence — *"I want my crew to get cited on ChatGPT and Perplexity for plumbing-Tel-Aviv queries."* I don't know what "Perplexity" is. I have to look it up. I don't want to look it up. Why am I picking which AI? You're the experts. You pick. Why am I doing your job?

Also "an animated pen-stroke" where I sign? Come on. I'm 47. I run a plumbing company. I don't need a pen-stroke animation. Just a button that says "Start." This is the kind of thing software people think is charming and I think is a slow loading screen.

### Monday Digest

*"Friendly email signed by your crew."*

Honest answer — I will read the first one. The third one I will archive. The sixth one I will filter to a folder. The ninth one I will unsubscribe from.

Mailchimp sends me 20 emails a month and I haven't opened one in a year. The bar to make me read your weekly email is HIGH. Make it three lines. Tell me what changed. Tell me if I need to do something. Don't sign it "your crew" — that's weird, I don't have a crew, I have a software subscription. Sign it "Beamix" or don't sign it.

Also — "story-form." No. I don't want a story. I have stories. My drainage emergency in Holon last Wednesday was a story. Your email is a status report. Make it a status report.

### The Weekly Board Report

Now this — wait. I read this part twice.

*"The artifact Sarah forwards to her CEO."*

I don't have a CEO. I AM the CEO. I'm reading this and I'm thinking — who is this for? Some bigger company than me? Or are you writing for the agency person at the bottom of the page (Yossi, you said), and I'm just along for the ride?

If I'm understanding right, the Weekly Board Report is a one-page PDF I can forward to someone. I might forward it to my husband Eli (who does the books). I would not forward it to "the board." There is no board. I would not forward it to my CEO. There is no CEO.

But — and this is interesting — I might forward it to my brother who keeps asking "is the website doing anything?" because he's an investor. Once a quarter. So yes, I would use this maybe 4 times a year. Not weekly.

Weekly is too much. Make it monthly. Or make it on-demand — "Want a one-page report to send to someone?" Button. I click it when I need it. That I would use.

### The score

"73 out of 100."

Okay. The truth is — when I saw "73" on the public scan I thought "okay, not great, not terrible, room to improve." That's all the math I did. The number worked. Not because I know what 73 means, but because it's a number, it's not 100, and I know what "room to improve" feels like.

If you tell me "82" next month I will think "good, going up." If you tell me "68" I will think "uh oh, what happened."

So the score is doing its job. Don't overthink it. Don't make me read methodology. I already trust it because it's a number. The only thing that would break it is if it bounces around — 73, 51, 78, 49 — then I stop trusting it.

What I really want isn't the score. What I really want is "phone calls last month: 47. Phone calls this month: 53." THAT is a number. The 73 is a proxy for that. If you can show me the proxy AND the real thing — phone calls, leads, form fills, whatever — I will believe you forever.

### The price — $189/month

Compared to what?

Compared to my Mailchimp ($45) — expensive.
Compared to my SEO consultant ($400) — cheap.
Compared to my Yellow Pages listing ($29) — expensive.
Compared to one extra plumbing job — three jobs a year pays for the whole thing.

Here's the truth: $189/mo is a "I'll try it for two months and see what happens" price. It's not "approve at the budget meeting" money. It's "Marina put it on the company card" money. Which is good for you.

But — and this is the SEO consultant trauma talking — if I don't see ANYTHING happen by month 2, I'll cancel. Not "consider canceling." Cancel. I have done it before. I will do it again.

### The "AI agent" framing

Mixed feelings.

On one hand — I like that it's not humans pretending to be doing work. The SEO consultant was humans pretending to be doing work. I'd rather pay $189 for software that does nothing than $400 for a person who does nothing. At least the software is honest.

On the other hand — "agents working autonomously." Autonomously? They're going to change my website without me looking? I read that part — "trust gradient" — and I had to read it three times.

Are you going to edit my website without asking? Are you going to write things on my Google profile? Are you going to email my customers? I need a list of things you will and will not do without asking. Right at the top. Not buried in a "trust tier" thing.

The fear is: I wake up, my website says something I didn't approve, a customer calls and asks why we're suddenly offering "24/7 emergency rooter service" which we are NOT, I have to apologize, refund, embarrassment. The cost of one bad agent action is so much higher than the benefit of ten good ones.

Default should be: agents draft, I approve, then it goes live. Only after months of me approving the same kind of thing should anything go automatic. And even then, send me a list every Friday — "here's what we did automatically this week, anything wrong?"

### "Agent attribution everywhere"

*"Every action carries the agent's signature. Click name → agent profile."*

Honestly? Noise. I don't care if Schema Doctor or Citation Fixer did it. I care if it worked. Tell me what changed and what the result was. The "which agent did it" is your internal accounting. Don't make it my problem.

Maybe — MAYBE — if something breaks, I want to know which one to yell at. But day to day? Just tell me what happened.

This feels like a feature designed by someone who is proud of having built 11 agents. Not a feature designed for me.

### "House Memory"

*"Every approval, rejection, margin note... stored as structured agent-account memory."*

Okay this is the one that made me pause.

On one side — yes, I want it to learn what I like and stop asking me dumb questions. That's good.

On the other side — "stored." Where? Who can see it? If I cancel, do you keep it? If I switch to a competitor, can I take it? "House Memory" sounds friendly but the actual word is "we have a database about your business that grows over time." Which is fine if you say it like that and tell me my rights.

Not creepy. But not nothing. Tell me clearly: this is your data, you can export it, you can delete it. Then I'm fine.

The "phase 2 — aggregated learning across accounts" part — *"Other businesses in your category solved this by..."* — that I actually like. If another plumber in Haifa figured something out, I want to know. As long as my data doesn't get shown to my competitor.

### "Public permalinks — default-public"

No. No no no.

Wait, let me make sure I understand. *"Every digest, every scan, every report has a permanent URL. Default-public."*

So if I get a weekly report that says "score dropped 8 points because Plumber X is beating you" — that has a public URL? Plumber X can find it? My competitor can read my weekly reports?

I read further — "per-user toggle to private (decision for Adam — see open questions)." So Adam hasn't decided yet. Let me decide for Adam: private by default. PRIVATE BY DEFAULT.

I am not sharing my weekly business performance on a public URL. I'm not sharing what's working and what's not. That's competitive intelligence — for THEM. The viral mechanic is your problem. My business is my business.

If you want viral — let me share specific things I'm proud of. "I improved 15 points this quarter — share?" Yes. I'd share that. But the default of "everything is public" is — sorry, this is the dumbest thing in the document. A 50-person business owner will close the tab the moment they understand this.

You're confusing "I'm proud" with "I'm happy to be naked in public."

## Section 2: The 5 things you'd actually need to keep paying after month 3

Here's what would make me renew. Specifically.

**1. The phone rang because of you.**

This is the only one that really matters. If at month 3 I can point to ONE phone call — one customer who said "I found you on ChatGPT" or "I asked AI about plumbers in Tel Aviv and you came up" — I renew for a year. I don't even ask the price.

You need to MAKE me see this. Don't just tell me my "score went up." Build a way for me to ask incoming customers "how did you find us" and tag the AI ones. Or hook into my phone system. Or send me a survey for me to send to new customers. SOMETHING that connects your work to my actual phone ringing.

Without this — even if my score goes from 50 to 95 — I might cancel because I can't tell if it's working in real life.

**2. I told my friend about it.**

If at month 2 I'm on a call with another business owner and I say "by the way, there's this thing I'm using for the ChatGPT problem" — you have me forever. Word of mouth is how I buy.

For me to say that, two things have to happen: (a) I have to feel something working, and (b) I have to be able to explain what it does in one sentence. "It makes my business show up in ChatGPT" is the sentence. Get me to say that sentence by month 2.

Not "AI Visibility Crew." That sentence will NEVER come out of my mouth.

**3. Six minutes a week, max.**

If I'm spending more than six minutes a week on this thing, I'm canceling. I have 11 fires today. You are not fire #1. You're fire #43.

Six minutes means: I open one email, I scan it, I either click "yes do it" on something or I close. That's it. No dashboard browsing. No exploring. No "agent profiles."

If your product requires me to log in and look around to get value — you've lost. The email IS the product. The dashboard is for when something goes wrong.

**4. Something shipped that I would never have done myself.**

This is the "SEO consultant" memory. The reason I canceled him was I couldn't tell what he did. With you — I need to be able to point at a thing and say "Beamix did that." A new FAQ page on my website. A fixed schema thing (whatever that is) that I can SEE in the email — "we added this to your site, here's the link." A response to a competitor I didn't know about.

Specific. Concrete. With a link. With a before-and-after. Every week.

If month 3 I can scroll through my email folder and see 12 specific things that Beamix did to my business — I renew. If I scroll and see 12 emails that say "everything is going great, your score is 78" — I cancel.

**5. The board report I send to Eli (or my brother).**

Wait — I think I'm coming around on the Board Report. Not weekly. But monthly.

Here's why: my brother (the investor) emails me every quarter and says "is the website doing anything?" Right now my answer is "I think so?" If I had a one-page Beamix report I could forward — "here's what's been happening, signed by these AI agents that work on it" — I look professional. I look like I'm running this business smartly. And he stops asking.

That alone might be worth $189. The "I look smart in front of my brother" feature.

So: rename Weekly Board Report → Monthly Owner Report (or just "The Monthly"). And let me forward it without making it public. And I'm in.

## Section 3: The 3 things in Frame 4 that confuse, annoy, or scare me

### 1. The Standing Order ceremony

The whole "you sign a written agreement, animated pen-stroke, persistent at top of /home, edits preserved as versioned letters" — I get what you're trying to do, you're trying to make it feel like hiring a real person.

But it doesn't feel like hiring a real person. It feels like signing the iTunes terms and conditions. Nobody reads those.

The harder problem: I don't KNOW what to put in my standing order. I'm not a strategist. If you ask me "what do you want your crew to do" my answer is "I don't know, what should they do? Aren't you the expert?" If you make me articulate my own marketing strategy upfront, you've made me do work I came to you to NOT do.

Do this instead: scan my site, scan my competitors, then YOU draft a standing order, then I just say yes/no/edit. Reverse the flow. Don't make me write it. Make me approve it.

And drop the "letters" framing. It's not a letter. It's a settings page.

### 2. The "Calm tone, dense information" thing — calm but full

I read this part — *"Calm comes from visual hierarchy and low urgency, not from absence of content."*

Okay sure. But the fold above is now: standing order excerpt, signed status sentence, score with activity ring, evidence strip with 3 cards, decision card, tier badge.

That's six things. Above the fold. On my phone (because that's where I'll open it the first time, because Marina forwarded the email).

That is not calm. That is a dashboard. Stripe is calm because Stripe does ONE THING and shows you that thing. You're trying to be calm AND show me everything happening. Pick one.

If I had to choose: show me ONE thing per visit. "This week, here is the most important thing." Don't make me triage six widgets. The whole reason I'm paying you is so I don't have to triage.

### 3. The agency person

I see Yossi mentioned a lot. *"Yossi at Scale tier. Yossi value-prop. Yossi gets free-form. Yossi white-label."*

I'm not Yossi. I'm Sarah. I run plumbing.

Reading this document, half the features are for Yossi. White-label. Per-client board reports. Free-form standing orders. Twenty clients on one subscription. That's a different product.

I have a real fear that you're going to build for Yossi (because Yossi pays $499 and I pay $189 and Yossi will tell more people) and end up making MY product worse — more complicated, more knobs, more "free-form" things I don't want.

If you build for me — single business, 47 employees, 6 minutes a week — I'm yours. If you build for Yossi, I'll be the awkward middle tier that gets neither the simple thing nor the powerful thing.

Decide who Beamix is for. If it's Yossi, say so. Then I know to look elsewhere.

## Section 4: What Beamix should ACTUALLY say to win me

Here's how to talk to me. Try this:

> **"When customers ask ChatGPT for a plumber in your area, does your name come up?**
>
> **Probably not. We fix that.**
>
> Here's how it works: we scan how AI search sees your business. We tell you what's broken — in plain English. Then our software fixes it for you, week by week. You approve what you want, ignore the rest.
>
> Once a month we send you a one-page report you can forward to your husband, your accountant, your investor — whoever asks if your website is doing anything.
>
> $189/month. Cancel anytime. First scan is free."

That's it. No "crew." No "11 agents." No "Standing Order." No "AI Visibility." No "GEO."

Just: the problem (you're invisible to ChatGPT), the solution (we fix it), the proof (monthly report), the price.

The reason this works for me:
- It says the problem in MY words ("ChatGPT," "plumber," "in your area")
- It says the solution in plain action verbs ("scan, tell you, fix")
- It respects that I'm busy ("you approve, ignore the rest")
- It addresses the trust thing without naming it ("monthly report you can forward")
- It puts the price near the promise — not buried — and "cancel anytime" tells me you're not afraid

What you have now reads like a product manager wrote it for an investor. What I want reads like a friend explaining it at a coffee shop.

Test: read your closing-pitch paragraph out loud. Now read mine. Which one sounds like a person? Which one sounds like a deck?

A few more things to drop:
- Drop "Visibility" as the word. Use "show up." "Get found." "Get cited." Working verbs.
- Drop the agent names from external marketing. They can live inside the product. But on the home page? "Schema Doctor" is going to confuse my office manager.
- Drop "GEO" entirely from anything I see. I'll never search for that. I'll search for "is my plumbing business in ChatGPT."
- Stop calling things "artifacts." It's a report. Or a page. Or an email.

The one piece of marketing that would actually convert me: a video. 60 seconds. Real plumber, real shop, owner says "I asked ChatGPT for a plumber in Tel Aviv and it didn't say my company. Then I tried Beamix. Three months later, it does. Here's my Monday email." That's the ad. Not a deck.
