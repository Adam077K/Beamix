# Frame 4 — The Visionary Seat
*Board review, Seat 4. Beamix product design direction.*
*Date: 2026-04-26*

---

## Section 1 — Critique of Frames 1, 2, 3

### Frame 1 — Managed Service ("the Wealthfront frame")

**Category it puts Beamix in.** Robo-advisor for AI search. The implicit comparison set is Wealthfront, Betterment, Found, Pilot, Vanta-on-autopilot. The product pretends to be a balance sheet that updates itself. The brand promise is *trust through invisibility*: the best service is the one you forget you have.

**Who it competes with.** Not Profound. Not Otterly. Not Athena. Frame 1 doesn't compete in the GEO category at all — it competes with the *idea of doing nothing*, which is a much harder competitor than another dashboard. Frame 1's actual rival is the SMB owner thinking "AI search will sort itself out, I have a website."

**5-year story.** Frame 1 ages into a financial-services-shaped company: monthly statements, fiduciary register, low churn, a customer who renews because cancelling is more cognitive load than paying. It's a good business. It's not a category-defining one. Five years deep, the ceiling is "the Bench.co of GEO" — a quietly profitable SaaS that no one writes a Stripe Press essay about. No one talks about it at dinner.

**Defensibility.** Strong on relational moat (switching costs from a customer who never opens the app), weak on product moat. Profound copies the email digest in a quarter. Athena adds an "automated recommendations" toggle. The wedge erodes because the product surface — one status, one number, one decision — is too thin to differentiate at the visible layer. There's nothing here a competitor screenshots and says "we can't ship that."

**Brand promise scalability.** Holds in marketing ("we do the work"), holds in product (one card, no thinking), holds in client-deliverable (a PDF that says "we did the work"). But scales *down*, not up. A logged-out visitor in five years doesn't get a sense of *ambition* from this brand — they get a sense of competence. Stripe didn't win by being the most competent payments company. They won by feeling like the future of money.

**Verdict on ambition.** Honestly: not ambitious enough. Frame 1 is brave in restraint, but its restraint is pre-categorial — it's the simplification you do when you're an incumbent reducing surface area, not the simplification you do when you're inventing a category. Wealthfront simplified an existing category (RIAs). Beamix is supposed to invent one. Frame 1 is the answer to "what if we made GEO boring?" That's a tactic, not a vision.

---

### Frame 2 — Operating Studio ("the artisan editor's studio")

**Category it puts Beamix in.** A craft tool for power users — Linear / Things 3 / iA Writer / Notion-for-experts. Notebook surfaces, Reasoning Receipts, Sentence Builders, card flips, the Letter, the Press. The product is structured as *prose that operates*. Beamix is the iA Writer of GEO.

**Who it competes with.** Other editorial-craft tools, not other GEO tools. Implicitly: anyone selling beautiful software to thoughtful operators. The fight isn't with Profound — it's with the customer's expectation that a $189/mo product should feel like Linear, not like another AI-slop dashboard.

**5-year story.** Frame 2 ages into a *cult*. A passionate, smaller-than-it-should-be userbase that loves the product the way people love Things 3 or HEY. There's a 70-tweet thread every quarter about the Reasoning Receipt. The press loves it. The TAM doesn't grow as fast as Profound's because the product asks too much of the user, and the SMB owner who hired Beamix to *not think about GEO* opens the Notebook, sees prose, and closes the tab. Five years deep, the ceiling is "the HEY of GEO" — beloved, niche, unshippable to the actual mass market.

**Defensibility.** Very high on craft moat. The Reasoning Receipt, Sentence Builder, and chip-in-prose grammar are genuinely hard to copy because they require taste discipline most teams don't have. But this is the *wrong* moat for the customer. Sarah, the SMB owner who didn't go to design school, doesn't even *perceive* the moat. She perceives "this looks complicated." The moat protects the product from competitors who don't matter (other artisans) and fails to protect it from competitors who do (other "we'll do it for you" GEO tools that are uglier but easier).

**Brand promise scalability.** Beautiful in marketing screenshots. Holds in /scan public (where the craft pays off — that's an acquisition surface, you're courting). Breaks in client-deliverable (Yossi's client doesn't want to read a Notebook entry from an agent named "Citation Fixer" — they want a one-page status with a logo). Doesn't scale across the customer's emotional journey: the same surface that delights week 1 becomes work week 12.

**Verdict on ambition.** This is craft fetish dressed as vision. Twelve distinctive moves competing to be the protagonist is the design equivalent of a sentence with twelve adjectives — the writer is showing off, not communicating. The moves are good *individually*. The composition is undisciplined. Frame 2 is what happens when you confuse "ambitious" with "elaborate." The most ambitious products in history (iPhone, Stripe Checkout, Linear's Cmd-K) are not elaborate. They are *singular*.

---

### Frame 3 — Calm by Default, Depth on Demand ("the locked frame")

**Category it puts Beamix in.** A managed service with optional craft layers. The honest reading: Frame 3 is Frame 1 with escape hatches. The signature move (One Status / One Decision / One Number) is a robo-advisor pattern. The depth scroll, the public scan, and the white-label PDF are concessions to the customer journey beyond week 1.

**Who it competes with.** Frame 3 hedges. Above the fold it competes with Wealthfront (clean, calm, trustworthy). Below the fold and on /scan it competes with Linear (depth, craft). On /reports it competes with consultancy deliverables (premium artifact). This is *strategically reasonable* — and it's also why Adam's instinct that Frame 3 doesn't feel category-defining is correct. Frame 3 is a portfolio of competent answers, not a singular bet.

**5-year story.** Frame 3 ages into a *good* company. Probably profitable. Probably $30-80M ARR. Probably acquired by Semrush or HubSpot in year 4 because it has nice retention numbers and a defensible workflow but no compounding asset that makes it a $1B independent business. The exit headline reads "Beamix acquired to add AI-search visibility to Semrush's SMB suite." Adam takes the meeting. Adam takes the deal. Adam regrets it for fifteen years.

**Defensibility.** Same problem as Frame 1, slightly better. The depth scroll and white-label artifact are nice but not protective. The Monday Morning Digest is the strongest compounding asset (relational, hard to leave) but its surface is just an email — Profound copies it in a sprint, and now we're competing on operational excellence, not on a product moat. The trust-tier router is a real architectural asset, but it's invisible to the customer; they perceive it as "fewer clicks," which is not a story.

**Brand promise scalability.** Better than Frame 1. The /scan public + /reports artifact + email digest form a passable brand surface. But it's not *coherent*. /scan is high-craft. /home is austere. /reports is editorial. The customer experiences three different products depending on which surface they're on. There's no single image that says "this is what Beamix is."

**The honest critique.** Frame 3 is the smart compromise. It's what a senior PM ships when the CEO says "be ambitious but don't break things." It's the Toyota Camry of product design — every decision is defensible, no decision is *moving*. Adam's intuition that it's "too austere at the surface" is half-right; the deeper problem is that Frame 3 has *no center of gravity*. It's not organized around a metaphor. It's organized around a triad (Status / Decision / Number) that is a *layout*, not a *worldview*. Layouts don't define categories. Worldviews do.

**Verdict.** Frame 3 is the safest of the three. It is also, paradoxically, the *least visionary*. Frame 1 had the courage of a single conviction (be invisible). Frame 2 had the courage of a single aesthetic (be artisanal). Frame 3 says "let's do both, calibrated by tier and surface." That is a roadmap, not a vision.

## Section 2 — Frame 4 Proposal: *The Live Company*

### The metaphor

**Beamix is a live operating company that runs in your browser.** Not a tool. Not a dashboard. Not a managed service. *A company.*

The image: when Sarah opens Beamix at 9:47 on a Tuesday, what she sees is not a UI — it's *the back office of a small firm she hired*. There is a roster of eleven specialists. There is a record of work they did this week. There is a digest from her account manager on Monday. There is a public page that anyone can visit to see the firm's work for her, like a contractor's portfolio. The app is the company's office. The email digest is her account manager. The white-label PDF is the firm's letterhead. The /scan public surface is the firm's storefront.

This is not a metaphor stretched over a SaaS. This is the actual *informational architecture* of a service firm — and Beamix is the first GEO product where that architecture is the product, not the marketing copy on the homepage.

The competitors are dashboards pretending to be services. Beamix is a *service rendered as software*.

The closest analogues:
- **Stripe Atlas** — incorporates your company; the product is the *act of becoming a company*, not a form.
- **Mercury** — banking that feels like a finance team, not a bank login.
- **Pilot** — bookkeeping rendered as a relationship with a bookkeeper, not a Quickbooks alternative.
- **The Browser Company's Arc** — software that has a *worldview* about what a browser should be, not just a feature set.

Beamix's claim, stated cleanly: *we are the GEO firm you would have hired, if a GEO firm existed at the right size and price.* The product is the firm.

### The signature move: **The Standing Order**

Frame 3's signature was One Status / One Decision / One Number — a layout. Frame 4's signature is a *concept*: **the Standing Order**.

A Standing Order is a written instruction the customer gives the firm — in plain English, in the customer's words — about *what they want done on their behalf*. It is the contract between Sarah and her crew, persisted at the top of the product, visible everywhere.

> *"Get me cited on ChatGPT and Perplexity for 'plumber Tel Aviv' and twenty queries like it. Don't change my brand voice without asking. Send me a digest on Monday."*

Three paragraphs. Hers. The crew works to it. Every action the agents take traces back to a clause in the Standing Order. Every digest opens with "this week, against your Standing Order, we did the following." Every flagged item in the Inbox says "this exceeds your Standing Order — your decision." Every white-label PDF for Yossi's clients begins with the client's Standing Order printed on the inside cover.

The Standing Order is not a settings page. It is the *constitution of the relationship*. It is the most important document in the product. It is editable, but every edit is logged. It scales from Sarah's three sentences to Yossi's per-client multi-page version. It is the artifact that makes Beamix feel like a firm and not a tool.

**Why this beats One Status / One Decision / One Number.** The triad is reactive — it tells the user what's true *now*. The Standing Order is generative — it tells the user, the agents, and any third party (Yossi's client, an auditor, a future Beamix employee) *what this relationship is for*. Triads are layouts. Standing Orders are worldviews.

Visually, the Standing Order lives at the top of /home as a **two-line excerpt** with a "Read the full order" link. It is rendered in InterDisplay 22px, slightly grey, like a quotation pulled from an old letter. It is referenced (never restated) at the top of every digest email. It is signed (date-stamped) at the bottom. When the user updates it, the previous version is preserved as a *received letter* — Beamix replies, in the digest: "We received your updated standing order on Tuesday. This week we worked under the new terms. Here's what changed."

That is the move Profound cannot ship in a sprint, because it requires the company to have decided what kind of company it is.

### The flywheel: **The House Memory**

Beamix gets better the more Sarah uses it because the product accumulates *house memory* — a structured, queryable record of every decision, correction, approval, and override in the relationship. Not a chat log. Not an audit trail. A *firm's institutional memory*, the way a real services firm remembers "this client doesn't like exclamation marks" and "this client always wants the report by Friday."

Concretely, House Memory is:

1. **Every approval and rejection is a training signal.** When Sarah rejects a Citation Fixer suggestion, the system asks (one sentence, optional): "What was wrong with this?" Her answer becomes a constraint on the agent for that account, forever. After 10 weeks, the agents have learned her voice well enough that the Inbox is empty most weeks because the agents pre-empt her objections.
2. **Every Standing Order edit is preserved as a versioned letter.** The crew has an archived correspondence. New agents (when added) read the entire history before doing work.
3. **Every weekly digest is an immutable artifact.** Sarah can scroll back twelve months and see exactly what was done in week 17. This is the *receipt of the relationship* — the equivalent of a long-term Stripe statement, but for work.
4. **Cross-account learning at the tier boundary.** Sarah's House Memory is hers. Yossi's House Memory spans his clients. Beamix's *aggregate* memory (anonymized, opt-in) becomes a corpus that powers a Phase 2 feature: *"Other firms in your category had this issue and solved it this way."*

The flywheel is structurally identical to GitHub's — every commit makes the platform more valuable than the sum of its features — except the commits are agent decisions and customer corrections, and the value compounds inside the customer's account, not across the platform.

This is also the answer to "why doesn't Sarah leave for the next cheaper tool?" — because she would have to start over with a firm that doesn't know her. The same reason she doesn't fire her accountant.

### The defensibility — what Profound can't copy in three years

Three things, in order of strength.

1. **The Standing Order as the unit of work.** This is a *worldview commitment*. Profound is built around prompts, queries, citations — atomic data. To adopt Standing Orders, Profound would have to rebuild around the unit of *agreed work* — which means reorganizing their entire product around the customer's intent, not the platform's data. They won't. They are a data company; we are a firm. Different gravity.
2. **House Memory as the compounding asset.** Twelve months of Sarah's corrections is not replicable by changing tools. The longer she's been with Beamix, the worse the alternatives feel. Profound can copy the surface; they cannot copy *her relationship history* with us.
3. **The composability of a firm — agents as colleagues, not features.** When Beamix adds the 12th agent, it doesn't ship a feature; it *hires* a specialist, with a name, scope, and standing-order clauses that govern its work. This framing means Beamix's product surface scales linearly with hires (the way a firm scales with headcount), not combinatorially with feature flags. Profound's product gets more complex as they add features. Beamix's product gets *deeper* as we add agents — same shape, more capacity. This is the architectural moat.

In plain English, three years deep, what Profound has is a better dashboard. What Beamix has is *a relationship with the customer that includes twelve months of correspondence, an institutional memory of every decision, and a written contract about what we're for*. One of these is software. The other is a company.

### The brand surface — the moment of clearest expression

**The single image that expresses Beamix's worldview is the Monday Digest.**

Not the /home page. Not the /scan public. The digest. Because the digest is the moment when the firm *speaks*, and what it says — in three sentences signed by name — is the entire brand promise rendered as a piece of writing.

The Monday Digest, as Frame 4 designs it, opens like this:

> *Monday, April 27.*
>
> *Against your standing order — get cited on ChatGPT and Perplexity for plumbing-Tel-Aviv queries — this week your crew shipped six fixes. Score: 73 → 78. ChatGPT now cites you on four new queries. Citation Fixer noticed a competitor pulled ahead on Wednesday and closed the gap by Friday. One thing exceeds your order: rewriting the homepage hero to match Perplexity's question pattern. Your decision.*
>
> *— Your crew*
>
> *[See the receipts](https://app.beamix.tech/digests/2026-04-27)*

That paragraph is the entire product's voice in seven sentences. It is signed. It is dated. It is referenced to the Standing Order. It links to a permanent receipt. The receipt is **a public-by-default page** the customer can share — that is the viral artifact the prior frames missed entirely. (More on this below.)

Render this digest in plaintext-with-typography (no images, no buttons, no boxes — actual letters, like email used to be), and you have a brand surface that no GEO competitor can imitate without rebuilding their company around the same worldview.

### The 5-year roadmap implication — why this scales 100x

Frame 1 caps at $30-80M ARR (managed-service moat, low TAM at SMB).
Frame 2 caps at $50-100M ARR (cult product, high CAC, low expansion).
Frame 3 caps at $80-150M ARR (the smart compromise — buyable, not buildable into a category leader).

Frame 4's ceiling is meaningfully higher because the metaphor *"a firm rendered as software"* is portable across categories. Once Beamix has shown that a Standing Order + House Memory + a roster of agents can replace a $150K/year SEO consulting retainer at $189/mo, the same architecture replaces:

- A $200K/year content-marketing agency → Beamix Content (Standing Order: "publish two posts a week in this voice, on these topics, distribute to these channels").
- A $300K/year reputation-management firm → Beamix Reputation (Standing Order: "monitor my brand on these surfaces, flag when X, escalate when Y").
- A $500K/year fractional-CMO retainer → Beamix CMO (Standing Order: "operate my marketing function within this budget toward these goals").

Each of these is a different vertical, but the *product architecture* is identical: the firm-as-software. Beamix becomes the platform on which AI-native services firms are built — a category bigger than GEO. This is the Stripe-Atlas-becomes-Stripe move: start narrow (incorporate a company), become the substrate for an entire vertical (Stripe for the internet economy).

Frames 1, 2, and 3 are products. Frame 4 is a platform thesis disguised as a GEO product.

### Public surfaces vs private surfaces — the missing layer

Frames 1-3 missed the most powerful surface: **public receipts.**

Every weekly digest, every scan result, every white-label PDF in Frame 4 has a **public permalink**. By default. Like a Notion page. Like a Substack issue. Like a GitHub commit. Sarah can share `app.beamix.tech/firms/sarahs-plumbing/digests/2026-04-27` with anyone — her business partner, an investor, a customer — and it renders as a beautifully-typeset, brand-consistent, read-only page that says *"this is what my GEO firm did this week."*

This is the **viral / share / publishable layer** that the prior frames missed. The mechanic is identical to Stripe's payment receipts (every transaction has a permalink), Linear's issue links (every issue is shareable), Vercel's deploy URLs (every deploy is a URL). Beamix's version: every digest is a URL, every scan is a URL, every report is a URL.

**Why this matters for category creation.** When a Beamix customer shares a digest URL, the recipient sees *a firm at work*, not a SaaS dashboard. The framing the recipient receives is "Sarah hired a GEO firm called Beamix and here is what they did this week." That framing is itself the marketing message. Every share is a brand impression that *ratifies the firm framing*. Profound's dashboards are not shareable in this way because they're not artifacts of work — they're snapshots of metrics. There's nothing to share.

The public layer is also Yossi's entire upsell story: at Scale, his clients receive digests at *their* permalinks, on *his* white-label, hosted at *his subdomain*. Yossi runs his consultancy on Beamix's public-receipt infrastructure. That is a $499/mo lock-in no competitor offers.

### The /home redesign — section by section

**Section 1 — The Standing Order excerpt (above the fold, always).**
Two lines pulled from Sarah's Standing Order, rendered in InterDisplay 22px, soft grey, with a small clock icon and "signed April 12" timestamp. A subtle "Read the full order →" link. This is the *first thing the user sees every time they visit*. It tells them what their crew is working toward. It changes only when they edit it.

**Section 2 — The signed status sentence.**
One sentence in Inter 28px, signed by the crew (literally — small "— your crew" attribution in muted Fraunces italic). "This week we shipped six fixes. Score is 78, up five. Nothing needs you." The sentence has a *voice*. It reads as a person speaking, not a system reporting. Color-coded by state (green/blue/amber).

**Section 3 — The number, with attribution.**
Score 78, in 72px Inter tabular numerals. To its right: a 12-week sparkline. *Below* the number: a single line — "calculated against 47 queries across ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, and Claude. Methodology →" The number is not bare; it is *justified*. This is what makes Beamix feel like a firm rendered as software: the number has a footnote.

**Section 4 — The Decision Card (conditional).**
If anything exceeds the Standing Order, one card with the agent's name, the question, and Approve / Modify / Decline. If nothing exceeds the Order: literally empty space. The empty state itself is signed: "Nothing exceeds your standing order this week. — your crew."

**Section 5 — The Crew at Work (live, optional, ambient).**
A small horizontal strip near the bottom of the fold: 11 small monogram circles, one per agent, each pulsing softly if active. Hover reveals "Citation Fixer is reviewing 3 competitor mentions." Click reveals the work in progress. This is the *firm's office, glimpsed through a window* — Sarah doesn't have to look, but if she does, the firm is alive. This replaces the "topbar status pill" of Frame 3 with something warmer and more grounded.

**Section 6 — The Receipts (below the fold).**
Reverse-chronological list of *digests, decisions, and scans* — every artifact the firm has produced, with permalink icons. This is the *correspondence file*. Sarah scrolls and sees twelve weeks of her firm's work, each row a clickable receipt. House Memory rendered as a list.

**Section 7 — Goal progress (kept from Frame 3, reframed).**
"You're 67% of the way to *get cited on ChatGPT for plumber Tel Aviv*." Reframed as: this is a clause in your Standing Order, here is the firm's progress against it. The metric is identical; the framing makes it part of the relationship, not a gamification stripe.

**What's killed.** Hand-drawn metric illustrations (Frame 2). The empty space hero (Frame 1 / Frame 3). The score-as-headline framing (Frame 3) — score is justified, not headlined. Anything that reads as *dashboard* rather than *correspondence with a firm*.

### The /onboarding redesign — the magic moment

Frame 3's onboarding is Vercel — fast, two steps, first scan live. Good but not category-defining. Frame 4's onboarding is **the Founding of the Firm.**

**Step 1 — Confirm the brief (≤30s).** From the /scan public flow, pre-filled. User confirms business URL, name, industry. One screen.

**Step 2 — Write your Standing Order (≤90s, the magic moment).** A single text area, Inter 18px, on a cream background, with a *guided-prompt skeleton* that types itself in slowly:

> *"I want my crew to ___________. The most important AI engines for me are ___________. The thing I never want changed without asking is ___________."*

The user fills in the blanks in their own words. There is no validation. No required fields. The text is theirs. They click "Sign and start the crew" and the system *animates the signature* — a small pen-stroke in soft blue that draws underneath the order. The order is now the constitution.

**Step 3 — The crew responds (the wow).** The page transitions to /home. The Standing Order excerpt is now in place at the top, with a fresh "signed today" timestamp. Below it, the signed status sentence types in: *"We received your standing order. The crew is reading your homepage now. We'll send you our first digest on Monday. — your crew."* The 11 agent monograms appear at the bottom of the fold one at a time, each with a one-line introduction: *"Schema Doctor — fixing structured data."* etc. Like a team standing up to introduce themselves on a new client's first day.

That is the moment Sarah says "I have never seen anything like this." Not because of animation. Because she just *signed a contract with a firm that responded by name*.

**What's killed.** Goal-picker with three options (Frame 3). Credits configuration (always). Vercel-pattern as headline (Frame 3) — Vercel pattern is correct as a *speed* benchmark, but Vercel is for developers; Beamix's customer is a small-business owner who needs a *human* metaphor at first contact, not a dev metaphor.

### The /crew rebuild — agents as colleagues

Frame 3 demoted /crew to "digest authoring + agent roster." That's correct as a tactical move and wrong as a metaphor. /crew is the **firm's directory**, the way a small firm has a People page on its website.

**Above the fold.** "Your crew. 11 specialists working under your standing order." Below, a roster — but not a card grid. A *people-page list*, like a small consultancy's About page: name, role, what they did this week (in their own voice — "I added 11 FAQ entries this week"), with a small headshot-replacement (a soft monogram in their assigned hue, not a mascot, not a cartoon — a *typographic personality*).

**Click an agent → the agent's page.** Their scope, their standing-order clauses, their full work history, their settings (cadence, autonomy level), their conversation log with the user (every Inbox decision involving them). This is the agent's employee file.

**The new mechanic.** Each agent has a **standing-order clause they answer to**. Citation Fixer: "answers to clauses 1, 3, 7." This makes the agent's accountability visible — and makes adding agents feel like *hiring*, not *enabling features*. When Beamix ships agent #12, the announcement is "we hired a Schema Specialist," not "we added a feature."

**For Yossi.** Same page, with a "compose digest for client X" affordance — but framed as "write your client's Monday note" rather than "white-label digest export."

**What's killed.** Card flips. Mascots. Per-agent monogram personality across 11 agents (the personality lives in their *writing*, not their visual). The "agents-as-features" temptation that Frame 3 didn't fully escape.

### The user-crew connection — beyond email digests

The prior frames had three connection points: email digest, Inbox approvals, and (in Frame 2) the Notebook. Frame 4 adds two category-defining mechanics:

**1. The Margin Note.**
On any digest, scan, or report (public or private), the user can *annotate*. Highlight a sentence, add a note: "I disagree with this — here's why." The note becomes part of House Memory. The agents read it. The next digest references it: "We saw your note on last week's digest about brand voice. We adjusted Citation Fixer's draft style accordingly."

This is the mechanic GitHub uses (review comments) and Notion uses (page comments) — applied to the firm-customer relationship. It is the *first GEO product where the customer can talk back to the work.* No competitor has anything resembling this.

**2. The Open Question.**
At the bottom of every digest: "Anything you want to ask your crew?" One text area. The user types a question in plain English. The next digest opens with the answer. This is asynchronous, not chat — but it's a real *correspondence* surface that scales. It also generates a corpus of questions that informs roadmap and that, at scale, becomes Beamix's institutional knowledge of what SMBs actually wonder about GEO.

These two together — Margin Note + Open Question — are the *shareable artifacts Beamix spawns* that the prior frames missed. The Twitter-to-Stripe-Atlas analog: a published Beamix digest with margin notes is a thing the customer *can publish on LinkedIn* as proof of how seriously they take their own marketing. We just gave Sarah's marketing operation a public surface area she didn't have before.

### The moat in plain English

**One sentence:**

> *Profound shows you what AI engines are saying about you. Beamix is the firm you hired to fix it — with a written agreement, an institutional memory, and a public record of every week's work.*

True today (we're the only product framed as a firm, not a tool). True in three years (the institutional memory and the public record compound; competitors can copy the surface, not the relationship). True in a sentence (the customer doesn't need to understand "GEO," "agents," or "citation prediction" — they understand *firm, agreement, memory, record*).

## Section 3 — The Vision in One Paragraph

> Beamix is the GEO firm rendered as software. When a small business hires us, they sign a Standing Order — three paragraphs in their own words about what they want done — and the crew goes to work under it. Eleven specialists, named and accountable, each answering to specific clauses of the agreement. Every Monday morning, the crew sends a digest signed in their own voice: what we did this week, what changed, what exceeds your order, what's next. Every digest is a permalink the customer can share. Every approval, every margin note, every correction enters the House Memory — the firm's institutional knowledge of how to work for this client, which compounds for as long as the relationship lasts. The competitors are dashboards pretending to be services. Beamix is a service rendered as software, and the difference shows up in every surface: in the signed status sentence, in the public receipt, in the year of correspondence the customer can scroll back through and *see* the work. We are not the iPhone of GEO. We are the *Stripe Atlas* of services — the substrate on which the next generation of AI-native firms are built, beginning with the one your crew runs for you.

## Section 4 — Open questions for the board

1. **PM seat:** What's the build cost of public-by-default permalinks (auth model, sharing controls, cache invalidation, abuse vectors)? If it doubles MVP scope, do we still ship them as a default — or is "public on toggle" acceptable for Phase 1, with the firm-framing intact?

2. **Designer seat:** The Standing Order needs to be visually *singular* — it can't read as a settings page. The brief is "rendered like a quotation pulled from an old letter, signed and dated." How do you build that without leaning on Fraunces in a way that contradicts the English-only Inter-stack lock? Is there a typographic move that signals "agreement" without becoming editorial?

3. **User Researcher seat:** Will SMB owners actually *write* a Standing Order? Frame 4 is bet on the premise that Sarah will type three paragraphs about what she wants — the same Sarah who wouldn't read the Reasoning Receipt in Frame 2. What's our evidence she'll do this? If she won't, can we ship a *guided assembler* (pick from menus, output reads like prose) that hits the same psychological commitment without requiring writing?

4. **PM seat:** The House Memory flywheel requires accumulated approvals to be useful. How do we make the product feel *better* in week 1 than competitors, when our flywheel hasn't started spinning? What's the Day-1-vs-Day-180 experience differential, and how do we sell the long arc to a customer who hasn't lived it yet?

5. **All seats:** Frame 4 is bet on a worldview ("Beamix is a firm, not a tool") rather than a feature set. If 12 months in, the worldview hasn't landed in the market — customers still describe us as "a GEO dashboard with email" — what's the kill-switch back to Frame 3?
