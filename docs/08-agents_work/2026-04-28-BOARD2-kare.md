# Beamix Design Review — Through Susan Kare's Lens
Date: 2026-04-28
Reviewer: Senior Designer applying Kare's symbol-system + pixel-discipline + memorability disciplines

---

## A — The 4-mark sigil system audit

A "sigil system" is a strong claim. Kare would ask the only question worth asking: do these four marks form a *family* — same parent, same hand, same job description at different sizes — or are they four different ideas that happen to ship in the same product? My read of §2 of the Design System: two of them are real symbols, one is an effect that has been promoted to a symbol, and one is not a mark at all.

**The Ring is the strongest of the four.** A 200px circle with a 30° gap and a Rough.js terminus dot is a real symbol, in the sense the original Mac Trash was a symbol — a recognizable form that compresses meaning (cycle, watch, openness) into one shape. The deterministic seed per user ID is a small, beautiful idea: the same jitter for the same user, every time, on every surface. That is exactly the discipline the Mac startup screen had — the Happy Mac was the same Happy Mac, not a generated one. Keep this rule everywhere.

But the Ring has a scale problem the spec has not faced. At 200px on /home, it works. At 96px on the Monthly Update PDF, it still works — the gap is large enough to read as deliberate. At 24×24 on the security page hero (where the wordmark+sigil lockup sits), what we are calling "the Ring" is already not the Ring; it is the cross/star sigil. **The spec is conflating two different marks under one name.** The Ring at hero scale and the small mark next to "Beamix" in the topbar are not the same symbol. Kare would force this clarification: the Ring is the *score frame* (and only that); the small mark next to the wordmark is the *Beamix sigil* (and that is the Seal). Do not muddle them. At 16×16 favicon size, the Ring does not survive — the gap goes away, the terminus dot disappears, and you have a circle. Plan for this. The favicon is the Seal, not the Ring.

**The Trace is not a symbol; it is a treatment.** A 1.5px Rough.js underline at 28% opacity, fading over 7,200,000ms — that is an *effect applied to text*, closer to a highlight color than a Lasso. The Lasso was a symbol because it was *recognizable as a thing*. The Trace has no shape; it is a property. Keep it, but demote it: it is a *behavior*, not a member of the sigil family. The four marks should be objects, not adjectives.

**The Seal is the most important mark you have, and it is under-specified.** §2.3 gives one sentence of geometry. The Mac Watch had a defined hour hand, a defined minute hand, a specific 8-pixel tilt that read as "1:25" because Kare cared. The Seal needs that level of specification — see §D.

**The Margin is not a mark. It is a layout slot.** A 24px column on the left edge of an artifact, full of accumulated agent-color circles, is a *zone* — a margin in the typographic sense, a shelf for fingerprints. Calling it the fourth member of the sigil family confuses the typology. The Margin is to the sigil family what the desktop pattern was to the original Mac icons: the surface they live on. Promote the agent monogram to a real member of the family; demote the Margin to "the place marks accumulate."

**Are these the right four?** No. The honest family is three objects + one container: **Ring · Seal · Monogram · Margin (as host).** The Trace is a behavior of text, not a mark. Make the system three marks plus their stage. That is how the original Mac iconography worked: a small set of objects, performed on the desktop. Beamix's stage is the cream paper Margin; its actors are the Ring, the Seal, and the Monogram.

## B — The 18 agent monograms as a set

Kare designed icons as sets first. The first icon contains the next 50. So the question is: open the spec to the 18 agents, look at all 18 monograms together, and ask — *is there a unifying logic*, or is this 18 separate stylings tied together with the words "Rough.js circle"?

Right now, it is the latter. The spec for CrewMonogram (§4.11) is: "Rough.js circle, roughness 0.8, fixed seed per agentType, agent's color, agent's initial centered." That is a *template*, not a set. The Mac icons were a set because they shared a *grammar* — same stroke weight, same pixel density, same tilt vocabulary. Beamix's monograms share only the circle frame and the color rule. The interior — a single letter — is doing all the discrimination.

Look what the letters force you into: Schema Doctor "S." Citation Fixer "C." Content Refresher also "C." Competitor Watch also "C." Three of your 18 agents start with C, and the spec has not faced this. The Rough.js seed makes each scribble *visually* unique, but the user reads the letter, not the scribble. Three Cs in three colors at 16×16 is not a set; it is a coincidence with cosmetic separation.

There are two ways out, and Kare would push you toward the harder one.

**The easy way: 2-letter monograms** (which the /home Crew at Work strip has already drifted into — see §3.5: "12px InterDisplay 500, white text"). Schema Doctor → SD. Citation Fixer → CF. Competitor Watch → CW. Content Refresher → CR. The collision goes away. The eye reads two letters in 200ms. Color becomes a secondary cue, not the only cue. This is what the spec is converging toward but has not committed to. **Recommendation: lock 2-letter monograms across all surfaces, in InterDisplay 500 caps, drawn under the same Rough.js seed-per-agent rule.** The single-letter version stops at 12px in the Margin column, where 2 letters cannot fit — and at that size, color is doing the work, which is fine. But 16px and up: two letters.

**The hard way — what Kare would do: 18 unique symbols.** Schema Doctor is a proofreader's bracket-and-pencil. Citation Fixer is a quotation mark with a chevron. FAQ Agent is a question mark drawn slightly off-true. Competitor Watch is a small eye, two arcs and a pupil. Trend Spotter is an ascending zigzag. Brand Voice Guard is a quote-and-shield. This is the route that puts Beamix in the same conversation as the Mac iconography.

The hard way is a 6-month design project, not the MVP move. **Ship 2-letter monograms for MVP, but commit on a roadmap to replace them with 18 unique glyphs by month 6.** Treat the 2-letter version as scaffolding. Otherwise it calcifies, and Beamix spends 10 years with monograms that are slightly cleverer than Slack avatars.

**The color assignment is half-systematic.** Seven agents have explicit colors (Schema Doctor #6366F1, Citation Fixer #10B981, FAQ Agent #F59E0B, Competitor Watch #EF4444, Trend Spotter #8B5CF6, Brand Voice Guard #EC4899, Content Refresher #0EA5E9). The remaining 11 "cycle through the palette." That is not a system; that is a placeholder. Eleven agents get arbitrary leftover colors. **Lock all 18 colors now**, and commit to the full table in the design system. Pick the colors with cultural readability in mind (the FAQ amber and Competitor red are good; some of the Tailwind defaults will not survive on cream paper at 12px). Test them next to each other at 12px in the Margin column, the smallest size they appear, and confirm they discriminate. Right now you are building a color-blob system at small sizes and a letter-system at large sizes; one of them is going to break.

**The memorability test.** Honestly: after two weeks, a customer recognizes 5 of 18 by mark alone; maybe 7 if daily. The other 11 read as "an agent acted" — a colored dot. That is what most icon sets achieve. Kare's bar was higher — Mac users could name all 30 system icons by month two. Clearing that bar requires the 18 unique glyphs. For MVP, the 2-letter system is honest scaffolding.

## C — The 11 EngineChip abbreviations

The decision to use 2-letter abbreviations instead of engine logos is correct, and the reasoning (licensing risk + visual consistency) is correct. Logos would import 11 different design languages into a system that is trying to have one. Kare would approve.

But the specific abbreviations need work, and "AI" is the bug. **AI Overviews → "AI" is a category-name collision** in a product literally about AI. A user reading "ChatGPT 78 · Claude 65 · AI 71" cannot parse what "AI" means without already knowing. Rename to "AO" (AI Overviews) and add it to the lock-list. This is a 30-second fix that prevents 6 months of customer-support questions.

The other ten abbreviations are mostly fine, but check **CG vs GR** at 11px caps with 0.06em tracking — Inter at small caps blurs C and G. **CG (ChatGPT)** would be safer as **GP** or **CT**. Open the spec, render the eleven side by side at 11px, and audit. (My instinct: GP, PX, GM, CL, AO, GR, YC, CP, MA, MI, DS — replace CG with GP, MS with CP for Copilot.)

**Single-letter monograms vs two-letter abbreviations.** Engines get 2 letters. Agents get 1 letter (or, per my §B, should also get 2). **The system should be consistent: agents and engines both use 2-letter monograms, both rendered with the same InterDisplay 500 caps treatment, both at 11–14px depending on context.** That symmetry is what makes a set feel like a set. Right now agents and engines have different rules, which means there are two systems instead of one.

**Should engines be anthropomorphized like agents?** No. Engines are utility; agents are colleagues. Agents wear the Rough.js circle (warm, hand-drawn). **Engines wear a clean rectangle** — `radius-chip` 8px, no Rough.js, no jitter, no per-engine seed. Hand-drawn discipline is reserved for *Beamix's* actors, not third-party services. We drew our crew; we did not draw OpenAI. Currently /scan Frame 4 renders engine bubbles in Rough.js (roughness 1.2, higher than the agent monograms). That is wrong. **Engines should be the one place on /scan where Rough.js is *suppressed*** — clean circles at 96px in the storyboard, crisp rectangles at 56px in EngineChip. The hand-drawn vocabulary belongs to Beamix.

The locked state — opacity 40%, padlock corner — is fine, but the symbol is doing nothing extra; color is doing all the work. Add a 1px dashed border on locked engines so the lock reads at 16×16 too. Pixel discipline.

## D — The Beamix Seal

The Seal is the most important mark in the system and the least specified. §2.3 gives one sentence of geometry: "derived from the Beamix logo star/cross shape, 16×16 to 32×32." That is not a spec; that is a wish. The Apple logo took 18 months to land. The Trash icon went through dozens of revisions. Beamix's Seal — the mark that signs every artifact, every Monthly Update, every Brief approval, every OG share card — got one sentence.

**Lock the geometry this week.** Specify total path length, stroke width at every size (not "1.5px" — different at 16, 20, 24, 32), corner roundness, gap proportion, optical center. The Seal at 16×16 should not be the Seal at 32×32 scaled down; it should be a separately-tuned 16×16 version, the way the Apple logo is tuned per size. Pixel discipline at small sizes.

**The Seal needs to survive flat (no animation).** §2.3 leans hard on `motion/seal-draw` (800ms stroke-dashoffset + 50ms settle) to give the seal authority. The PDF top header, OG share card, and email header are *static* contexts — no animation possible. Test the Seal at 24×24 with no motion, against a printed-broadsheet page corner. If it does not carry weight without the draw-on, the geometry is too thin.

**The 800ms draw + 50ms settle is too cute.** A seal is *stamped*, not *traced*. **Invert the curve:** path draws in 240ms (fast — stamping), 100ms settle hold, then 200ms ink-bleed (stroke deepens to full opacity from 60%). Total 540ms. Faster, more confident, metaphor-accurate. The current 800ms reads like the Seal is unsure whether it is allowed to be there.

**Cultural readability for Hebrew users.** A star/cross-derived mark needs careful audit. The Star of David is six-pointed; the Beamix mark must not accidentally resemble it. "Cross" carries Christian valence in some readings. **Lock the geometry as a 4-pointed asterisk or a chamfered plus sign — neither cross nor star — and document this in the brand guidelines explicitly.** Pinterest's "P" was specifically chosen to be neither a pin nor a letter but a hybrid mark. Make the same decision deliberately.

**Trim the contexts.** Seven appearances; two are weak. "Inbox approve button hover" — the seal as foreshadowing-on-hover is precious; animate on the click, not the hover. "/scan public artifact footer" — a permalink page, not an artifact being signed. **Cut to 5: PDF top, PDF bottom, Brief approval, OG share card, Monthly Update email header.** Scarcity is what makes a seal valuable.

## E — Pixel discipline at small sizes

The original Mac icons lived at 16×16 and 32×32. Every dot was deliberate. Beamix has the luxury of retina, but the cognitive recognition still happens at small sizes — the favicon, the topbar mark, the Margin monogram, the Inbox row icon. Walk through them.

**Activity Ring at favicon size.** It cannot survive. The 30° gap collapses; the terminus dot disappears. **The favicon is not the Ring; the favicon is the Seal at 16×16.** Make this explicit in the spec.

**Agent monograms at 12–16px in the Margin column.** Per §B, the Rough.js circle + single letter system breaks at 12px — letters become unreadable. The Margin marks at 12–16px should be **color circles only, no letters** — solid agent-color discs with a 1.5px outline in the same agent color, no interior text. The user learns the colors by repetition. At 32px and up, the 2-letter monogram appears. Make this size-conditional rule explicit: under 16px = color disc; 16–32px = 2-letter monogram; above 48px = 2-letter monogram with name label below. Three sizes, three treatments, no hybrid.

**StatusToken dots at 6px.** §4.1 specifies a 6×6 colored dot. At 6px, the dot is two retina pixels of color. The shape does not matter; the position matters. Confirm the dot is *consistently* 6px on every status pill — green Healthy, blue Acting, red NeedsYou — and that the colors all clear WCAG 3:1 against the pill background. Test on the cream paper register specifically. The semantic-red `--color-needs-you` may not pass on cream.

**The Beamix wordmark + sigil mark at 24px in the topbar.** §3 of /security shows the lockup at "24px tall total." Lock the proportional relationship: how many px between mark and wordmark, what is the mark's width relative to the cap-height of "Beamix," and is the mark left of the wordmark or right? At 24px, the relationship is the design — get it wrong and it reads as an SVG and a word, not a logo. Kare cared about this exact relationship in Pinterest's P + wordmark; spend a day on it.

## F — Workflow Builder node grammar

Each agent node is 240×88, with a 24px charcoal header, a 16×16 monogram, agent name, config summary, and status token. Trigger nodes are the same frame in cream paper. Action nodes are 240×64, ink-3 header.

This is closer to a real set than the agent monograms are, because the *frame* discriminates by color and the *content* discriminates by monogram. Three node families (trigger, agent, action), three header colors, one shared frame. That is family logic.

But the frame is too tall. **240×88 for an agent node is generous to the point of looking bureaucratic.** Compare to React Flow exemplars (Retool, Make, n8n at their best): the most-respected DAG canvases use ~180–200px wide × 56–64px tall nodes. Beamix's 88px tall reads as form-field, not symbol. **Recommendation: tighten to 220×72**, drop the config-summary line into the inspector when selected (it is already truncated, and the user is going to click into the inspector anyway — the on-canvas summary is doing 30% of its job). 72px tall = header 24 + body 48 (one tight row of monogram + name + status).

**Connection handles need a state.** §6 says handles are "12×12 invisible default → 6×6 brand-blue dot on hover." The handle has no shape until interaction. Kare would push: handles should be visible at low priority (1px ink-4 ring, 6×6) all the time, and brighten on hover. A handle that is invisible until you go looking for it is a discoverability bug, not a design choice. n8n got this right; Make got it wrong; copy n8n.

**The deferred-to-MVP-1.5 event trigger placeholder** is a small but important set decision. The "Coming MVP-1.5" badge in 11px caps on `paper-elev` works — it is the same badge treatment used elsewhere in the system. **Confirm the badge is reused exactly, same px, same tracking, same ink-4 color**, across every "deferred" surface. One `<DisabledBadge variant="coming-soon">` component. If the team builds two slightly-different "coming soon" treatments, the family logic cracks.

## G — Direct response to other designer voices

The prior Senior Designer's claim that the 4-mark sigil system is "Beamix's visual identity" is the right ambition with the wrong execution. A visual identity is what you see in 200ms, across surfaces, and recognize without reading the wordmark. The Apple logo is a visual identity. Pinterest's P is a visual identity. Notion's clean monolinear icon set is a visual identity. **Beamix's four "marks" are not yet that** — they are a Ring (which works), a behavior pretending to be a mark (the Trace), a Seal that is under-specified, and a layout slot pretending to be a mark (the Margin). Calling the system a "visual identity" before locking the Seal geometry is jumping the order. Lock the Seal first, ship it consistently, and the identity will follow. *Then* put the four-mark language in the brand book.

The Master Designer's "craft IS moat when coupled to product purpose" position is correct in principle and dangerous in practice. Craft as moat is how Linear, Stripe, and Anthropic earned their reputations. But craft is only moat if it is *compounding* — every screen makes the system more itself, not more elaborate. Beamix is at the moment where the temptation is to *add* (a fifth mark, a sub-monogram for engine variants, a tiny sigil on every section heading). **Resist this.** Kare's discipline was to *subtract* until what remained was unimpeachable. The Mac shipped with maybe 30 icons. Linear's icon set is small. Beamix should ship MVP with three locked marks (Ring, Seal, Monogram), one clear behavior (Trace), one container (Margin), and *nothing else marked*. The temptation to add a section-divider rule, a "decision card" sigil, a per-status icon, a Beamie character — refuse all of it for now. Every refusal makes the locked set carry more. That is how craft compounds into moat. Adding before locking does not produce moat; it produces decoration.

There is a third voice worth answering: the Rams review (its file sits next to this one). Rams is right that the TopbarStatus dot is the strongest component in the system. Kare agrees — and would extend it: the dot is strong *because* it has done what Kare's icons did, which is reduce a complex state ("any of 18 agents is currently working on something") to a 6-pixel symbol that is unmistakable. Build more components like the dot. Build none that are more elaborate.

## H — The MoMA-collection version of Beamix's symbol system

The Susan Kare collection at MoMA includes the original Mac icon sketches on graph paper — pixel-by-pixel decisions, taped to a wall. Those icons are in MoMA not because they are pretty; they were *the first time* a computer told a person, in a glance, what was happening, with 256 pixels. The artifact in the museum is the *system*.

The equivalent move for Beamix is not a clever single icon. It is the rule that **the Rough.js seed for each agent is derived from the agent's identity**, not from a render call. The deterministic-seed rule — already in the spec, almost in passing — is the move that, fully followed, becomes a museum artifact 30 years from now.

Every agent in Beamix has a *fingerprint*: a Rough.js path generated from a fixed seed derived from the agent's UUID. That fingerprint is the agent's monogram circle. It is also the agent's signature on the Margin. It is also the underline-path (the Trace) under text the agent wrote. It is the small mark the agent leaves on the Monthly Update beside its actions. Across every surface — /home, /inbox, the PDF, the email, the OG card, the workflow node, the agent profile page — the *same drawn shape* recurs, agent by agent. A user on Beamix for two years has seen Schema Doctor's exact scribble 3,000 times. They recognize it the way Mac users recognize the Trash icon — instantly, pre-cognitively, as a friend.

**That is the move.** Not a new icon. The discipline of saying: each agent has *one* hand-drawn signature, fixed at agent creation, used everywhere. Never re-rendered. Never randomized. Never "freshened up in v2." The signature is the agent.

Three things must lock to ship this in MoMA shape:

1. **The seed-to-path function is part of the brand spec, not the codebase.** Document `seed(agentUuid) → path` as a brand decision; changing it across versions is forbidden the way changing the Apple logo is forbidden.
2. **The fingerprint appears at every scale, in every register.** The 96px hero monogram is the same path as the 12px Margin mark — same seed, scaled. Currently the system implies different seeds for monogram vs Trace; **unify under one path**, scaled and cropped per context.
3. **The set is not 18; it is the *function*.** The team does not design 18 monograms one by one. They design *one rule* that produces 18 fingerprints from 18 UUIDs. That rule is the artifact. That rule is what gets archived.

Craft as moat, in Kare's sense: a small, consistent, generative system that compounds across every surface for a decade. If Beamix ships this, the brand becomes uncopyable — not because the icons are pretty (they are), but because the *generating function* is the brand, and copying the function is copying the company. That is what gets in MoMA: the function, not the file.

## Closing — what the symbol system is missing

The Seal needs locked geometry and a faster animation curve. The 18 agent monograms need to go to 2-letter or commit to 18 unique glyphs, and the color table needs to lock all 18 now. The deterministic Rough.js seed-per-agent rule, already present in the spec, needs to become the spine of the system — one fingerprint per agent, used everywhere, never re-rendered.
